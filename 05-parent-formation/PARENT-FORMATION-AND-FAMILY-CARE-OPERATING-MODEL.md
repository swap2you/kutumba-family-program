# Purpose of this document

This document converts KUTUMBA’s parent-track vision into an implementable system. It defines what adults learn, how sessions work, how household practice is supported, what mentors may and may not do, how confidential matters are handled, how progress is reviewed, and how the work remains within devotional and educational boundaries.

It is designed for a small founding cohort of approximately five to six families, normally ten to twelve adults, meeting on Friday evenings within the wider KUTUMBA program. The model can scale, but it deliberately avoids bureaucracy that would delay a safe pilot.

# Executive decisions

| **Decision** | **Operating consequence** |
| --- | --- |
| **Outcome, not attendance** | The parent track measures changed household practice, relational conduct, and reliability—not mere presence, eloquence, disclosure, or devotional appearance. |
| **Application-led teaching** | Each lesson moves from a real family tension to śāstra, reflection, rehearsal, one household commitment, and review. |
| **Small commitments** | Families use observable, time-bounded saṅkalpas with a fallback version. Failed weeks trigger redesign, not shame. |
| **No compelled disclosure** | Adults may pass. Written or anonymous reflection is valid. Emotional intensity is not treated as evidence of growth. |
| **Education, not treatment** | KUTUMBA does not provide therapy, medical care, legal advice, addiction treatment, or marital adjudication. |
| **Controlled care** | Sensitive issues move from the group to a designated care or safety pathway. Immediate safety overrides ordinary confidentiality. |
| **Mentors have limits** | Mentors support routines and participation; they do not become spiritual controllers, therapists, investigators, lenders, or secret allies. |
| **One system for adults and children** | Parents study the same central theme as children and model the practices children are being asked to learn. |
| **Progressive commitment** | Advancement is gradual and private. Completion does not confer teaching authority, initiation eligibility, or automatic leadership. |
| **Review before scale** | The founding pilot is evaluated after every active cycle before enrollment or service responsibilities expand. |

# 1. Parent-track charter

The parent track forms adults who can understand Kṛṣṇa conscious principles, apply them in ordinary household life, and create a safe devotional culture in which children observe their parents learning, chanting, serving, apologizing, regulating speech, and accepting correction.

## 1.1 Purpose

- Help adults understand foundational Kṛṣṇa conscious principles in language connected to real family responsibilities.

- Build realistic personal and family devotional routines that can survive work pressure, school schedules, illness, travel, and imperfect motivation.

- Train communication, anger regulation, confidentiality, correction, apology, repair, and cooperation as learnable devotional practices.

- Help parents model the conduct they expect from children rather than outsourcing spiritual formation to a weekly class.

- Provide graduated support through facilitators, mentor families, and private review while preserving adult agency.

- Prepare families for stronger temple participation, service, deeper study, and—where appropriate—authorized future pathways such as Bhakti Śāstrī and initiation processes.

- Protect the program from gossip, spiritual coercion, emotional performance, amateur counseling, and unmanaged safeguarding risk.

## 1.2 Completion profile

A parent completing the three-year pathway should increasingly be able to:

- explain foundational philosophy without speculation;

- maintain a meaningful personal practice and a realistic family devotional anchor;

- offer food, read, chant, attend festivals, and serve with increasing steadiness;

- communicate truthfully and beneficially without normalizing agitation, contempt, gossip, or public humiliation;

- correct children calmly, proportionately, privately when possible, and with a clear path to repair;

- cooperate with a spouse or co-parent without using scripture, leaders, or children as leverage;

- recognize when a problem exceeds KUTUMBA’s role and use an appropriate referral pathway;

- participate reliably in service without neglecting health, livelihood, children, or essential household obligations;

- support newer families without controlling them.

# 3. Educational and care boundaries

The parent track must be useful enough to change behavior but narrow enough to remain safe. The correct boundary is education, formation, ordinary support, and referral—not diagnosis, adjudication, or treatment.

## 3.1 What KUTUMBA may teach

- devotional communication and speech discipline;

- anger interruption and ordinary emotional self-regulation;

- clear requests, listening, apology, repair, and household planning;

- parenting skills, positive correction, routines, media agreements, and family meetings;

- home chanting, reading, food offering, service, worship preparation, and festival participation;

- scriptural principles concerning responsibility, cooperation, simplicity, regulation, and devotional household life;

- how to identify a concern, protect privacy, and seek qualified support.

## 3.2 What KUTUMBA may not do

- decide which spouse is right or pressure reconciliation, separation, or obedience;

- investigate abuse, trauma, infidelity, addiction, criminal behavior, or child-protection allegations;

- diagnose mental-health, developmental, medical, or learning conditions;

- provide psychotherapy, addiction treatment, legal advice, financial advice, or medical treatment;

- promise that devotional practice alone will resolve a clinical, legal, or safety problem;

- compel disclosure, demand confession, or interpret tears, silence, reluctance, or doubt;

- use guru, śāstra, initiation, children, service, or community standing as leverage;

- circulate confidential information as “care,” “warning,” or “prayer request.”

| *“Austerity of speech consists in speaking words that are truthful, pleasing, beneficial, and not agitating to others.”* |

*Primary devotional anchors used in this model include Bhagavad-gītā 3.21, 6.17, 9.27, and 17.15; Nectar of Instruction 1 and 4; relevant household-life teachings in Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam; and the approved KUTUMBA source hierarchy.*

## 3.3 Immediate referral triggers

- current danger, violence, stalking, coercive control, threats, weapon access, or fear of going home;

- suspected child abuse, neglect, sexual abuse, exploitation, grooming, or unsafe adult-child conduct;

- suicidal thoughts, self-harm, threats to harm others, severe disorientation, or acute psychiatric crisis;

- serious addiction, overdose risk, withdrawal danger, or intoxication that threatens safety;

- medical emergency or a condition requiring clinical evaluation;

- criminal, immigration, custody, financial, or legal issues requiring licensed advice;

- a facilitator or mentor conflict of interest, boundary breach, retaliation, or misuse of confidential information.

# 4. Foreseeable failure modes and controls

| **Failure mode** | **What it looks like** | **Control** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Lecture creep** | Prem-kī-Kathā becomes a 35–45 minute discourse. | Use a visible timer; hard stop at 20 minutes; co-facilitator intervenes. |
| **Spouse prosecution** | Adults use discussion to build a case against an absent spouse. | Require first-person language; redirect to controllable behavior; move sensitive matters out of group. |
| **Emotional performance** | Dramatic disclosure is praised as deeper spirituality. | Passing is allowed; use private writing; praise practice and repair, not exposure. |
| **Mentor overreach** | Mentor becomes counselor, authority, lender, or secret ally. | Written role, training, one-to-two family limit, supervision, reassignment route. |
| **False accountability** | Families report what leaders want to hear. | Private status options; no rankings; normalize redesign and partial completion. |
| **Overloading** | Enthusiastic families accept too many services and practices. | One weekly program; capacity check; household consent; staged commitment. |
| **Gossip disguised as care** | Private information spreads among families or leaders. | Need-to-know rule; no informal forwarding; incident response for breaches. |
| **Unsafe spiritualization** | Clinical, legal, or abuse concerns are answered only with chanting or tolerance. | Referral triggers, current directory, trained care/safety lead, documented escalation. |
| **Parental hypocrisy** | Adults demand devices, chanting, and manners they do not model. | Every lesson asks what children observed in the parent’s conduct. |
| **Curriculum improvisation** | Facilitators add speculative philosophy or personal ideology. | Approved guide, source citations, lesson review, correction recorded in repository. |
| **Data leakage** | Mentor notes, children’s details, or disclosures enter Git or shared Drive. | Data classification, minimum collection, restricted storage, deletion schedule. |
| **Scale before stability** | Program adds families before facilitators and safety systems work. | Cycle-gate enrollment; capacity threshold; pilot defects resolved first. |

# 5. The KUTUMBA adult-learning model

Many adults in the target cohort are busy, experienced in worldly responsibilities, uneven in devotional knowledge, and resistant to passive lecture formats. The method must respect adult agency while refusing vagueness. The solution is a repeatable practice cycle anchored in śāstra and active application.

*Figure 2. KUTUMBA Practice Cycle*

## 5.1 The six-stage Practice Cycle

| **Stage** | **Required function** |
| --- | --- |
| **1. Reality** | Begin with a recognizable household tension. Adults must see why the principle matters before abstract explanation. |
| **2. Śāstra** | Study one primary passage or incident with enough context to prevent misuse. Avoid quotation piles. |
| **3. Reflection** | Examine one’s own conduct, assumptions, habits, and controllable choices—not the defects of absent people. |
| **4. Rehearsal** | Practise the sentence, routine, decision, or correction method in the room. |
| **5. Saṅkalpa** | Choose one observable action with days, time, duration, participants, likely obstacle, and fallback version. |
| **6. Review** | At the next meeting mark complete, partial, or not completed; identify the obstacle; keep, reduce, replace, or expand. |

*The use of active practice rather than lecture-only delivery is consistent with broad learning research showing stronger outcomes from active learning. The KUTUMBA model applies that principle cautiously to adult devotional formation; it does not claim that one education study validates every family intervention.*

## 5.2 Adult-learning design rules

- Relevant before abstract: establish the family-life problem before presenting the principle.

- One governing principle per session: secondary sources support rather than compete.

- Agency with accountability: adults choose disclosure and practice size, but covenant and safety rules remain binding.

- Skill before aspiration: practise what to say or do, not only what to value.

- Minimum viable practice: create a version that survives pressure.

- Retrieval and review: begin the next session by recalling and evaluating the prior commitment.

- Varied participation: speaking, writing, pair practice, anonymous questions, demonstration, and observation all count.

- No amateur psychology: facilitators describe behavior and choices, not diagnoses or hidden motives.

- No gender stereotyping: cases should not assume that one spouse always leads, earns, cooks, disciplines, or resists.

- No false equivalence: ordinary conflict can be discussed; coercion, violence, abuse, or threat moves to safety pathways.

## 5.3 Session evidence package

Every parent lesson should contain the following controlled artifacts:

| ☐  primary Śrīla Prabhupāda source and context note; | ☐  one-sentence governing principle; |

# 6. The 20-minute Prem-kī-Kathā method

Prem-kī-Kathā is the bridge between shared worship and parallel formation. It creates recognition and desire to apply; it is not the complete adult lesson and must not absorb the discussion period.

*Figure 3. Prem-kī-Kathā timing and purpose*

## 6.1 Required script architecture

| **Time** | **Element** | **Delivery requirement** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **0–2 minutes** | Family tension | State a recognizable, anonymized situation. Do not target a participating family. |
| **2–6 minutes** | Scriptural window | Present one verse, passage, or incident with the minimum context needed for accurate use. |
| **6–10 minutes** | Governing principle | State one sentence that can guide action. Clarify what the principle does not mean. |
| **10–13 minutes** | Analogy | Use one familiar comparison that clarifies the principle; avoid entertainment for its own sake. |
| **13–16 minutes** | Household application | Give a minimum application for a struggling family and a stronger option for an established family. |
| **16–18 minutes** | Silent reflection | Adults write privately. Passing and non-disclosure are explicitly permitted. |
| **18–20 minutes** | Transition | Ask one question that opens the guided parent session; stop on time. |

## 6.2 Example mini-script

|  | **FAMILY TENSION** |
| --- | --- |

Scriptural window: Bhagavad-gītā 3.21 teaches that others follow the example set by a leading person. Bhagavad-gītā 17.15 adds that disciplined speech is truthful, beneficial, pleasing, and nonagitating. The facilitator gives only the context required for the lesson and does not use the verses to claim that parents must never set firm boundaries.

|  | **GOVERNING PRINCIPLE** |
| --- | --- |

Analogy: a thermostat changes the environment; a thermometer only reacts to it. A parent who merely reflects the household’s agitation cannot guide the emotional climate.

Minimum application: pause for ninety seconds, lower the voice, and state one clear preparation request. Stronger application: agree on a device parking place and a ten-minute transition routine before the program.

Silent reflection: “What lesson did my conduct teach this week, regardless of what my words taught?” Transition question: “Which part of the situation was under the parent’s direct control?”

## 6.3 Speaker controls

- Use no more than four simple slides.

- Do not exceed one central story, one principle, and one analogy.

- Do not identify or indirectly describe a current family.

- Do not disclose mentor conversations or safeguarding matters.

- Do not generalize negatively about spouses, generations, cultures, professions, or children.

- Do not use guilt regarding guru, initiation, children, service, or community status.

- Do not introduce an unreviewed worship practice, speculative teaching, or internet quotation.

- End at twenty minutes even when the content is unfinished; revise the script afterward.

# 7. The 40-minute guided parent discussion

The discussion is structured to prevent domination, spouse criticism, and unproductive storytelling. It uses observation, case analysis, application, rehearsal, and commitment.

| **Minutes** | **Phase** | **Facilitator function** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **0–5** | Observe the text | What happened? What words or actions stand out? What is explicitly stated? What is not being claimed? |
| **5–15** | Examine the case | Identify facts, assumptions, competing responsibilities, avoidable mistakes, and safety boundaries. |
| **15–25** | Apply the principle | What can the parent control? What would worsen the situation? What is a firm but nonhumiliating response? |
| **25–33** | Rehearse | Practise a sentence, request, apology, boundary, routine, or repair step. |
| **33–38** | Choose application | Select a realistic saṅkalpa with normal and fallback versions. |
| **38–40** | Close | Summarize one philosophical principle, one relational skill, and one household action. |

## 7.1 Question ladder

| **Level** | **Question type** |
| --- | --- |
| **Discovery** | What facts do we know? What does the passage actually say? |
| **Meaning** | What principle explains the tension? What misuse must be avoided? |
| **Self-examination** | Where do I show this pattern? What am I expecting from others that I do not model? |
| **Choice** | Which part is under my control? What could I do in the next ten minutes? |
| **Rehearsal** | What exact sentence or sequence would I use? |
| **Commitment** | When, where, how long, with whom, and what is the fallback? |
| **Review** | What helped, what obstructed, and what will change next week? |

## 7.2 Discussion ground rules

- Speak from “I”; do not prosecute an absent spouse or child.

- Passing is permitted without explanation.

- Do not diagnose or assign motives.

- Do not give unsolicited marital, legal, medical, or psychological advice.

- Do not repeat another family’s story outside the authorized care route.

- Challenge ideas without humiliating people.

- Do not compare rounds, children, finances, knowledge, home standards, or service.

- The facilitator may stop a discussion that is unsafe, dominating, speculative, or outside scope.

- Safety matters are reported privately; they are not put to a group vote.

- The goal is practice and repair—not winning an argument or displaying knowledge.

## 7.3 Facilitator intervention phrases

| **Situation** | **Approved facilitator language** |
| --- | --- |
| **Redirect spouse criticism** | “Please bring this back to your own controllable response rather than evaluating someone who is not in the room.” |
| **Stop diagnosis** | “We can describe the behavior and impact, but we cannot diagnose the person.” |
| **Protect privacy** | “That detail is too personal for the group. We can discuss the general principle or speak privately through the care route.” |
| **Allow passing** | “Passing is a complete answer. You may use the written reflection instead.” |
| **Stop unsafe advice** | “This may require qualified professional or safety support; we should not solve it in the group.” |
| **Limit domination** | “I am going to pause here and hear from someone who has not spoken.” |
| **Return to śāstra** | “Which part of our assigned source supports that conclusion?” |
| **Close on time** | “We have the principle and action. The unresolved details belong in the next revision, not in an extended session.” |

# 8. Case-study method

A good case is realistic, incomplete, and morally complex enough to require judgment. It should not contain an obvious villain or secretly describe a participating family.

## 8.1 Case construction rules

- Use fictional names and alter identifying details.

- Include competing legitimate responsibilities such as work, sleep, school, caregiving, health, and service.

- Separate observable facts from assumptions.

- Include at least one choice available in the next ten minutes and one longer-term practice.

- Name the educational boundary and any safety trigger.

- Avoid gender stereotypes and cultural caricatures.

- Do not resolve every case with “chant more.” Include planning, communication, rest, referral, or skill practice where appropriate.

- End with self-application rather than judgment of the fictional family.

## 8.2 Core case bank

| **Case** | **Situation** | **Learning focus** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Two-speed couple** | One spouse wants a daily home program; the other is willing to attend only occasionally. | Invitation without coercion; separate shared minimum from personal practice. |
| **Friday exhaustion** | Both adults finish work late and repeatedly arrive stressed and unprepared. | Regulation, simplification, and realistic transitions. |
| **Do what I say** | A parent demands chanting while remaining on the phone. | Parental modeling and shared standards. |
| **Public correction** | A child disrupts kīrtana and is loudly criticized before everyone. | Private, proportionate correction and repair. |
| **Screen conflict** | Children resist device-free prasāda because adults check messages throughout meals. | Family media agreement and adult example. |
| **Temple-car gossip** | The family discusses another devotee’s marriage and children on the drive home. | Speech discipline, confidentiality, and repair. |
| **Takeout evening** | The family is too tired to cook and abandons all remembrance or offering practice. | Minimum viable devotional routine and approved food-offering guidance. |
| **Service overload** | One parent accepts several services while the other carries household duties. | Consent, capacity, and household responsibility. |
| **Child resistance** | A child says KUTUMBA and temple programs are boring. | Curiosity, relationship, age-appropriate choice, and meaningful service. |
| **Teen doubt** | A teenager asks difficult questions and the parent labels it rebellion. | Listening, intellectual honesty, and referral to qualified teachers. |
| **Extended family** | Relatives feel judged by the family’s new practices. | Respectful boundaries without superiority. |
| **Financial pressure** | A family feels guilty because it cannot donate or attend every event. | Material responsibility, voluntary service, and no comparison. |
| **Unequal discipline** | One adult is strict; the other reverses consequences in front of the child. | Private co-parent planning and consistent minimum standards. |
| **Angry apology** | A parent says “sorry” but adds blame and demands immediate forgiveness. | Complete apology and patient repair. |
| **Private message** | A mentor begins receiving late-night messages from one spouse about the marriage. | Mentor boundaries and transfer to appropriate care. |
| **Oversharing circle** | A participant gives detailed information about a child’s diagnosis and spouse’s behavior. | Facilitator interruption, privacy, and consent. |
| **New family comparison** | A late-joining family feels inferior to experienced families. | Setu pathway, self-comparison, and starter practices. |
| **Festival burnout** | The family attends several events, children lose sleep, and Friday becomes conflict-heavy. | Regulation, protected breaks, and one-principal-program rule. |

## 8.3 Standard case-card questions

1. What facts do we actually know?

1. What assumptions are the adults making?

1. Which responsibilities are legitimate?

1. What conduct is clearly unhelpful or unsafe?

1. What part can each adult control?

1. What scriptural principle applies?

1. What should not be concluded from that principle?

1. What could be said or done in the next ten minutes?

1. What longer-term household practice might help?

1. Does this require education, confidential care, or external professional support?

# 9. Safe participation and the rejection of forced sharing

The adult track should create honest reflection without making the group perform vulnerability. Adults are not required to disclose private struggles to prove sincerity, belonging, or advancement.

## 9.1 Acceptable participation modes

- Speak in the full group.

- Speak in a pair or triad.

- Write privately.

- Submit an anonymous question.

- Observe a role-play.

- Practise a neutral fictional scenario.

- Pass without explanation.

- Speak only about the principle and planned action.

## 9.2 Warning signs of emotional performance

- Facilitators praise dramatic disclosure more than quiet consistency.

- Participants feel obliged to match another person’s testimony.

- Tears or silence are interpreted publicly.

- Detailed marital or child information is treated as “honesty.”

- Leaders retell disclosures as inspirational examples.

- Adults use self-criticism to seek reassurance rather than choose an action.

- Public commitments are inflated beyond household capacity.

## 9.3 Trauma-aware facilitation without becoming therapy

KUTUMBA can apply basic safety principles—choice, predictability, clear boundaries, noncoercion, respectful language, and referral—without attempting trauma treatment. Facilitators do not ask participants to relive traumatic experiences, identify diagnoses, or process clinical symptoms in the group.

# 10. Parent-child bonding

Children are more likely to receive devotional instruction from adults with whom they experience attention, affection, fairness, and repair. Bonding is therefore not an optional social extra; it is part of the learning environment. It must not become permissiveness, manipulation, or forced intimacy.

## 10.1 The 5–20–1 rhythm

| **Rhythm** | **Minimum standard** |
| --- | --- |
| **Daily: 5 minutes** | Undistracted attention; one genuine question; listen without immediate correction; appreciation or affection; a natural Kṛṣṇa connection when appropriate. |
| **Weekly: 20 minutes** | Child chooses an approved shared activity such as story, music, art, cooking, altar care, nature walk, instrument practice, service, or devotional game. |
| **Monthly: 1 memory** | Create one shared devotional memory through cow service, temple visit, cooking, festival preparation, japa walk, family presentation, or helping another family. |

## 10.2 Connection rules

- The parent participates rather than supervising from a phone.

- The five-minute connection is not an interrogation about school, chanting, or behavior.

- The child’s choice is bounded by safety, age, budget, and family values.

- Parents do not demand emotional disclosure from children.

- Affection is not withdrawn as a disciplinary tool.

- Photographs and social posts are not the purpose of the activity.

# 12. Communication, anger, speech, and gossip

The governing devotional standard is not merely “say what is true.” Bhagavad-gītā 17.15 combines truthfulness with benefit, nonagitation, and disciplined scriptural speech. Nectar of Instruction 1 places speech, mind, and anger among the urges to be controlled. The parent track converts these principles into an interruptible household process.

## 12.1 The PAUSE response

| **Step** | **Action** |
| --- | --- |
| **P — Pause the body** | Stop speaking, lower volume, create safe distance, and avoid following someone through the house to continue the conflict. |
| **A — Assess** | Is anyone unsafe? Am I correcting the present behavior or unloading accumulated frustration? Must this be handled now? |
| **U — Understand** | Hear one explanation without interruption. Understanding is not the same as approving. |
| **S — State the standard** | Use one clear behavioral sentence; avoid character labels, historical accusations, and multiple commands. |
| **E — Encourage repair** | Ask what must happen now to make the situation safer or more respectful; return to the relationship after consequence. |

## 12.2 Approved language

| **Situation** | **Sample wording** |
| --- | --- |
| **Unsafe action** | “That action is unsafe. Stop and come with me.” |
| **Disrespectful speech** | “We speak respectfully here. Please try the sentence again.” |
| **Need for reset** | “You are not being removed from the family. You are taking a reset from this activity.” |
| **Delayed discussion** | “I am too agitated to answer well. We will return to this at 7:30.” |
| **Repair** | “What happened, who was affected, and what can you do to repair it?” |
| **Parental error** | “I shouted. That was wrong and made it harder for you to hear the instruction. I am sorry. I will repeat it calmly.” |
| **Request to spouse** | “I would like us to protect fifteen minutes on Sunday for family practice. Which time would interfere least with your schedule?” |

## 12.3 Language that KUTUMBA rejects

- “You are a bad devotee.”

- “Kṛṣṇa will punish you.”

- “Why can’t you be like that child?”

- “You always embarrass us.”

- “Everyone agrees that you are the problem.”

- Labels such as lazy, useless, demonic, stupid, hopeless, ungrateful, or spiritually fallen.

- Public sarcasm, threats of abandonment, or invoking leaders to frighten a child or spouse.

## 12.4 Gossip decision test

| **Test** | **Question** |
| --- | --- |
| **Ownership** | Is this information mine to share? |
| **Awareness** | Is the person present, aware, or consenting? |
| **Purpose** | Is there a legitimate action, protection, or service reason? |
| **Recipient** | Am I speaking to someone authorized and able to help? |
| **Integrity** | Would I speak the same way if the person entered the room? |
| **Minimum necessary** | Am I sharing only the facts required for the action? |

# 13. Parenting as a learnable art

KUTUMBA treats parenting as a sequence of observable skills rather than a fixed personality trait. The aim is neither harsh control nor permissiveness. Parents learn to teach, supervise, correct, apply related consequences, and repair the relationship.

## 13.1 Skill progression

1. Observe and describe behavior accurately.

1. Connect before demanding when the situation permits.

1. State the expectation in concrete terms.

1. Demonstrate and rehearse the expected behavior.

1. Notice effort and improvement without excessive praise or comparison.

1. Correct privately when possible and immediately when safety requires.

1. Use a proportionate, related consequence.

1. Allow a path back into the activity.

1. Repair after conflict and review repeated patterns.

1. Seek qualified help when behavior, development, health, or safety exceeds ordinary support.

## 13.2 Correction standard

| **Standard** | **Meaning** |
| --- | --- |
| **Calm enough to understand** | The adult does not need to feel calm, but must be regulated enough to communicate safely. |
| **Behavior-specific** | Describe the action; do not define the child’s identity. |
| **Private when possible** | Public intervention is for immediate safety or disruption; instruction and consequences move private. |
| **Brief** | One standard and one next step; do not deliver a sermon during dysregulation. |
| **Proportionate** | The consequence fits the conduct, age, understanding, and pattern. |
| **Related** | Repair, restore, practise, pause, or lose access connected to the behavior. |
| **Restorative** | The child knows how to return to the family or activity. |
| **Documented only when necessary** | Program incidents follow the safety procedure; ordinary parenting moments are not turned into records. |

## 13.3 Positive discipline and safeguarding alignment

Current American Academy of Pediatrics parent guidance emphasizes calm teaching, modeling, clear limits, attention to good behavior, and nonphysical strategies. KUTUMBA’s internal standard is stricter for program activities: no physical punishment, no public humiliation, no threatening spiritual consequences, and no adult disciplining a child outside the designated program process.

# 14. Marriage and household cooperation boundaries

The parent track may teach cooperation skills. It does not govern private marriages, determine fault, or create religious pressure between spouses. One spouse’s enthusiasm does not grant authority to impose every preferred practice on the other.

## 14.1 Teachable cooperation skills

- Making a specific request rather than a global accusation.

- Listening and summarizing before responding.

- Dividing preparation, childcare, transport, cooking, and service responsibilities.

- Recognizing invisible or unequal household labor.

- Discussing children privately rather than contradicting each other publicly.

- Agreeing on a shared minimum while allowing individual devotional practices.

- Apologizing, repairing, and revising an unrealistic plan.

- Protecting sleep, employment, medical care, and essential family obligations.

- Allowing different rates of spiritual development without mockery or coercion.

## 14.2 The household cooperation meeting

| **Step** | **Household action** |
| --- | --- |
| **1. Appreciate** | Name one contribution from the other adult. |
| **2. Review facts** | Calendar, work hours, children’s needs, health, service, transport, and food. |
| **3. Name one pressure** | Avoid a list of historical grievances. |
| **4. Make one request** | Specific, time-bounded, and negotiable. |
| **5. Agree on minimum** | Define the smallest shared practice or responsibility. |
| **6. Assign ownership** | Who prepares, reminds, transports, cleans, or leads? |
| **7. Set review time** | Revisit after one or two weeks rather than arguing daily. |

## 14.3 Prohibited uses of KUTUMBA

- Publicly correcting or embarrassing a spouse.

- Quoting a spouse’s private disclosure.

- Asking the group or mentor to decide who is right.

- Using the mentor to pressure the reluctant spouse.

- Invoking guru, initiation, children, or community standing to compel compliance.

- Creating a secret alliance between a mentor and one spouse.

- Presenting one cultural division of roles as mandatory for every family.

- Encouraging a person to remain in danger in the name of tolerance or family unity.

# 15. Devotional routines for working families

The routine must survive real life. A plan that depends on ideal mornings, abundant free time, equal enthusiasm, and perfect children is not a plan. Bhagavad-gītā 6.17 supports regulation in eating, recreation, work, sleep, and wakefulness rather than unmanaged extremes.

## 15.1 Three operating levels

| **Level** | **Frequency** | **Core pattern** | **Typical duration** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Level 1 — Starter** | Three days each week | 2 minutes settling/prayer + 5 minutes chanting + one verse, paragraph, or short story + one sentence of reflection | Approximately 10 minutes |
| **Level 2 — Steady** | Five days each week | 10 minutes chanting + 5–10 minutes reading/hearing + brief family prayer + regular offering of a designated meal | Approximately 15–25 minutes |
| **Level 3 — Established** | Most days | Individual adult sādhana according to capacity + protected family chanting/reading + consistent offerings + weekly service + temple/festival participation | Family-specific |

## 15.2 Routine-design rules

- Attach the practice to an existing event such as breakfast, dinner, school return, or bedtime.

- Use the same location and prepare books, beads, instruments, or offering items beforehand.

- Start shorter than the family believes it can manage.

- Stop before children are chronically exhausted or resentful.

- Define a normal version and a fallback version.

- Do not turn one missed day into a moral crisis.

- Review the routine after six weeks and after major schedule changes.

- Separate the family minimum from each adult’s personal practice.

- Do not add multiple new practices in the same week.

- Protect planned rest weeks and family emergencies.

## 15.3 Implementation-intention format

Research on implementation intentions supports converting a goal into an “if–then” plan that specifies the situation and response. KUTUMBA uses a simplified form:

|  | **PLAN FORMULA** |
| --- | --- |

Example: “After dinner on Monday, Wednesday, and Saturday, we will chant together in the living room for eight minutes. If we return home late, we will chant for three minutes before bedtime.”

# 17. Offering food in the home

Bhagavad-gītā 9.27 provides the integrating principle that food and ordinary activity can be offered to Kṛṣṇa. The parent track teaches a simple household workflow. Exact mantras, altar standards, and worship procedures remain under the approved KUTUMBA worship system and qualified ISKCON guidance.

## 17.1 Progressive pathway

| **Stage** | **Household practice** |
| --- | --- |
| **Stage 1** | Offer one designated home-prepared meal several times each week. |
| **Stage 2** | Offer one principal meal each day. |
| **Stage 3** | Offer all suitable home-prepared meals consistently. |
| **Stage 4** | Strengthen cleanliness, planning, ingredient awareness, gratitude, and children’s age-appropriate participation. |

## 17.2 Simple workflow

1. Plan a simple suitable meal and approved ingredients.

1. Maintain personal and kitchen cleanliness.

1. Avoid tasting before offering.

1. Place a portion on the designated offering plate.

1. Follow the approved offering procedure.

1. Wait for the agreed period and keep offered/unoffered food practices clear.

1. Return and distribute the prasāda respectfully.

1. Involve children through safe, age-appropriate preparation, plate setup, prayer, serving, or cleanup.

## 17.3 Boundaries and busy-evening design

- Simplify the menu rather than abandon the practice.

- Maintain a small list of quick no-onion/no-garlic meals that meet household standards.

- Do not independently invent rules for restaurant food, packaged items, fasting, or Deity worship.

- Do not criticize another family’s food or compare offerings.

- Allergy, medical, and dietary needs remain legitimate and must be handled safely.

- The offering process is devotional worship, not a child performance or household competition.

# 18. Screen and media culture

The objective is not technological purity. It is to protect attention, relationships, sleep, learning, privacy, and devotional remembrance. Current AAP guidance has shifted beyond simple time limits toward quality, context, conversation, healthy modeling, and a family media plan.

# 19. Material responsibility and spiritual growth

KUTUMBA rejects two weak extremes: using work and family duties as a permanent excuse for no spiritual practice, and neglecting livelihood, health, sleep, marriage, children, or essential bills in the name of spiritual enthusiasm. A responsible householder integrates duty and devotion through regulation, offering, simplicity, and service.

## 19.1 Capacity guardrails

- No KUTUMBA commitment should require neglecting essential employment or legal obligations.

- Unsafe sleep deprivation is not treated as spiritual advancement.

- Children may not be left unsupervised so adults can attend or serve.

- Essential bills, necessary medical care, and food security take priority over optional donations or travel.

- One spouse may not pressure the other to donate, host, or accept service.

- Families do not attend multiple KUTUMBA events every week; festivals and outings replace the regular program when scheduled.

- Service is reduced when the household is in crisis, illness, bereavement, job transition, or caregiving overload.

- Growth should create greater clarity, simplicity, responsibility, and service—not unmanaged burnout.

## 19.2 Capacity check before accepting service

| **Check** | **Question** |
| --- | --- |
| **Time** | Do we have the actual preparation and recovery time? |
| **Consent** | Have affected adults agreed, not merely remained silent? |
| **Children** | Who will supervise and how will sleep/school be protected? |
| **Health** | Is the task safe given current physical and mental capacity? |
| **Money** | Does it create avoidable cost or financial pressure? |
| **Skill** | Have we been trained and can we ask for help? |

# 20. Confidential care versus public discussion

Loving devotional exchange can include confidential inquiry and revealing one’s mind, but that does not place every private matter in a group circle. KUTUMBA uses a three-zone model that protects both honest discussion and safety.

*Figure 4. Confidentiality and care triage*

## 20.1 Green, amber, and red zones

| **Zone** | **Examples** | **Response** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Green — group-safe** | General routines; anonymized cases; ordinary struggles with consistency; communication skills; parenting questions; devotional doubts stated without identifying third parties. | Facilitated group discussion. |
| **Amber — voluntary and limited** | Personal habit; frustration; minor disagreement; missed commitment; ordinary motivation difficulty. The participant controls detail and may pass. | Brief sharing, private writing, or mentor check-in. |
| **Red — private/refer** | Detailed marital accusations; abuse; threats; self-harm; trauma; addiction; child diagnosis; legal, medical, immigration, custody, or detailed financial information. | Stop group detail; activate care/safety/referral route. |

## 20.2 Confidentiality promise

KUTUMBA should use precise language rather than promising absolute secrecy:

| *“We will limit personal information to people who need it for care, safety, or required action. We cannot promise secrecy when a child or adult may be unsafe, when reporting is legally required, or when urgent professional help is needed.”* |
| --- |

## 20.3 Information-handling rules

- Do not record group disclosures.

- Do not store private mentor notes, child information, medical details, or family contact data in Git.

- Do not post sensitive information in broad group chats, email chains, shared drives, or presentation tools.

- Do not forward screenshots of private conversations.

- Record only operationally necessary facts, dates, actions, and recipients.

- Restrict access based on role and review access periodically.

- Do not use a disclosure in lesson examples, even with names removed, unless explicit informed permission and safety review exist.

- Do not promise secrecy where safety or reporting duties apply.

- Delete temporary notes according to the approved retention schedule.

- Report a confidentiality breach promptly to the Program Director and Safety/Care Lead.

## 20.4 Minimum incident record

| **Field** | **Record requirement** |
| --- | --- |
| **Date/time** | When the concern was observed or disclosed. |
| **Reporter/receiver** | Names and roles of people directly involved in receiving the concern. |
| **Observable facts** | Exact words or behavior when material; no diagnosis, speculation, or character judgment. |
| **Immediate safety action** | What was done to protect the child/adult or secure emergency help. |
| **External/internal contact** | Who was contacted, when, by what approved route, and any confirmation number. |
| **Information shared** | Only what was necessary and with whom. |
| **Next required step** | Named owner and due date; no informal investigation. |
| **Storage location** | Restricted approved record system, never the public curriculum repository. |

# 23. Resistant-parent engagement

Resistance is data, not automatically disobedience. It may arise from exhaustion, prior religious harm, fear of control, philosophical doubt, social discomfort, child concerns, unequal labor, overscheduling, language barriers, or feeling judged by experienced devotees.

## 23.1 Engagement pathway

| **Step** | **Action** |
| --- | --- |
| **1. Listen** | Ask privately: “What part of the program feels least useful or most difficult?” |

| **3. Offer a low-risk entry point** | Kīrtana and prasāda; one family activity; one service; one short recording; one ten-minute practice; one cycle trial. |
| **4. Run a four-week experiment** | Choose a practice linked to the parent’s stated concern. |
| **5. Review honestly** | Ask whether anything improved; do not manufacture success. |
| **6. Respect adult choice** | One spouse may participate more; do not publicly pursue the reluctant spouse. |
| **7. Enforce covenant boundaries** | Skepticism is allowed; repeated disruption, ridicule, safety violations, or undermining children’s protection is not. |

## 23.2 Engagement options by barrier

| **Barrier** | **Practical response** |
| --- | --- |
| **Time/exhaustion** | Starter routine; fallback version; arrive for only the core program during a temporary overload; reduce service. |

# 24. Gradual commitment pathway

Commitment develops through observed stability and voluntary responsibility. The pathway is developmental, not a rank system. Families may pause, repeat a stage, or progress differently across domains.

*Figure 5. Gradual commitment pathway*

## 24.1 Stage definitions

| **Stage** | **Definition** |
| --- | --- |
| **0 — Observer** | Attends up to two sessions; learns the flow; no service or practice commitment. |
| **1 — Setu family** | Completes orientation, covenant, foundational philosophy/culture modules, forms, and one starter practice; receives mentor support. |
| **2 — Anchored family** | Maintains one regular chanting or reading anchor, begins offering food, follows safety/communication standards, and attends with reasonable consistency. |
| **3 — Practising family** | Maintains several home practices, demonstrates improving speech and repair, and participates in appropriate service. |
| **4 — Responsible serving family** | Completes service training, fulfils roles reliably, communicates early, and does not neglect household responsibilities. |
| **5 — Readiness and deeper study** | Demonstrates stable practice, growing comprehension of Śrīla Prabhupāda’s books, temple integration, and understanding of formal study/initiation seriousness. |
| **6 — Mentor/facilitator consideration** | Requires character, discretion, philosophical reliability, training, supervision, safeguarding compliance, and explicit leadership approval. |

## 24.2 Movement criteria

- Practice is reasonably consistent for the family’s actual circumstances.

- The family can recover after disruption instead of abandoning the system.

- Adults respect confidentiality, safety, and anti-gossip standards.

- Service commitments are reliable and appropriately sized.

- Parents increasingly model what they ask of children.

- The family accepts correction without retaliation or faction-building.

- No unresolved safety or serious boundary concern blocks increased responsibility.

- The family understands that stage movement is not initiation or spiritual status.

# 25. Assessment and progress review

Assessment is descriptive, private, and multidimensional. No single score defines a family. The aim is to choose the next helpful practice and identify risks, not to create spiritual competition.

*Figure 6. Six private assessment domains*

## 25.1 Six domains

| **Domain** | **Review question** |
| --- | --- |
| **Understanding** | Can the parent explain the central principle accurately, in context, and without speculation? |
| **Personal practice** | Is there a meaningful personal devotional anchor appropriate to current capacity? |
| **Family practice** | Does the family chant, read, offer food, worship, serve, or remember Kṛṣṇa together with reasonable regularity? |
| **Speech and relationships** | Is the adult improving in anger regulation, truthful/beneficial speech, confidentiality, apology, and repair? |
| **Parenting and modeling** | Does the parent demonstrate the conduct expected from children and correct without humiliation? |
| **Service and reliability** | Does the adult fulfil or renegotiate commitments without overloading the household? |

## 25.2 Four-level rubric

| **Level** | **Description** |
| --- | --- |
| **0 — Not examined** | The area has not been considered, taught, or attempted. |
| **1 — Experimenting** | The family has begun but is inconsistent, unclear, or dependent on reminders. |
| **2 — Developing** | The practice is regular enough to affect household life but breaks under pressure. |
| **3 — Stable and self-correcting** | The practice is reasonably established and the family can recover, redesign, and continue after disruption. |

|  | **NO COMPOSITE SCORE** |
| --- | --- |

## 25.3 Review cadence

| **Cadence** | **Review** |
| --- | --- |
| **Weekly** | One-minute private saṅkalpa review. |
| **End of each six-week active cycle** | Short reflection and mentor/facilitator conversation. |
| **Six months** | Structured family review: strongest change, least stable area, household barrier, relationship impact, child response, service capacity, next priority. |
| **Annually** | Private development conversation and revised family practice plan. |
| **Before role expansion** | Separate suitability review for service, mentoring, facilitation, worship, or children’s responsibilities. |

## 25.4 Program-level indicators

Program evaluation should aggregate only what is necessary and avoid sensitive family detail. Useful indicators include:

- percentage of active families selecting a realistic saṅkalpa;

- percentage reporting complete, partial, or redesigned—not individual names;

- facilitator adherence to the 20-minute and 40-minute structures;

- number of lessons requiring philosophical or safeguarding correction;

- mentor check-ins completed within role;

- retention at cycle boundaries rather than week-to-week pressure;

- participation spread—whether a few adults dominate;

- service reliability and early cancellation communication;

- confidentiality or boundary incidents and corrective action;

- parent-reported usefulness, pressure, belonging, and clarity;

- child-track observations concerning parental modeling and family follow-through, without public comparison.

## 25.5 Measures the program must not use

- Public round counts or devotional practice leaderboards.

- Children’s memorization or behavior rankings.

- Amount donated, cost of prasāda, size of home, or number of temple visits as spiritual worth.

- Emotional disclosure as evidence of sincerity.

- Attendance alone as readiness for service or leadership.

- Private marital information as a progress metric.

- Initiation recommendation as a promised graduation outcome.

# 27. First six-month parent formation overlay

The first six months cover three seven-week cycles. Five active instructional/application weeks are followed by one integration week and one protected off week. The following overlay aligns the adult track with the central curriculum themes.

## 27.1 Cycle 1 — Identity and the Human Problem

| **Week/theme** | **Parent focus** | **Home application** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Why are we here?** | Spiritual education cannot be outsourced to a weekly teacher. | Hold a ten-minute family purpose meeting. |
| **2. I am not this body** | Correct behavior without defining a child by body, achievement, diagnosis, or mistake. | Remove one recurring negative label from household speech. |
| **3. The nature of the soul** | Dignity, patience, and loving the person without approving every action. | Five minutes of undistracted connection with each child. |
| **4. Human life is rare** | The family calendar teaches children what adults truly value. | Reduce one low-value weekly activity or protect one devotional anchor. |
| **5. Temporary world** | Unrealistic expectations placed on marriage, children, career, possessions, or entertainment. | One gratitude and expectation-reset conversation. |

| --- | --- | --- |

## 27.3 Cycle 3 — Kṛṣṇa, Guru, and the Process of Bhakti

| **Week/theme** | **Parent focus** | **Home application** |

# 28. Staffing model

For ten to twelve adults, the parent track requires at least one trained lead facilitator, one co-facilitator, and a designated care/safety contact outside the ordinary discussion role. One person may initially hold multiple administrative roles, but safety authority and confidential escalation should not depend entirely on the lead speaker.

## 28.1 Minimum roles

| **Role** | **Minimum responsibility** |
| --- | --- |
| **Parent-Track Lead** | Owns curriculum delivery, facilitator calibration, cycle review, and parent-track changes. |
| **Lead Facilitator** | Prepares and delivers the assigned lesson; protects source, timing, and discussion boundaries. |
| **Co-facilitator** | Tracks time, participation spread, boundary risks, anonymous questions, and improvement notes; takes over when the lead has a conflict. |
| **Care/Safety Contact** | Receives sensitive concerns, owns the current referral directory, and coordinates approved escalation. |
| **Curriculum/Philosophy Reviewer** | Checks source accuracy, application, and absence of speculation before release. |
| **Mentor Coordinator** | Selects, trains, assigns, supervises, and reassigns mentor families. |
| **Documentation Owner** | Versions lesson packs, publishes approved material, and keeps private information out of the repository. |

## 28.2 Group-size controls

| **Condition** | **Operating response** |
| --- | --- |
| **Up to 12 adults** | One facilitator + one co-facilitator; full-group discussion with pairs/triads. |
| **13–14 adults** | Use structured turns and split rehearsal groups; monitor domination and privacy. |
| **More than 14 regular adults** | Normally split into two parent discussion groups with trained facilitators. |
| **More than 2 families per mentor** | Add qualified mentors or replace weekly mentor contact with centralized structured check-ins. |
| **Insufficient safeguarding capacity** | Do not expand enrollment; resolve staffing and training first. |

- Do not create narrative profiles of families.

- Do not store spiritual judgments or speculative mental-health labels.

- Separate child medical/safety records from curriculum and attendance data.

- Use role-based access and review it when volunteers change.

- Define retention and secure deletion before collecting sensitive information.

- Obtain explicit consent for media and explain where approved images may appear.

- When exporting or sharing a report, remove names and rare details that can re-identify a family.

# 34. Pilot review

The pilot review should distinguish curriculum defects, facilitation defects, operating defects, and individual family-care needs. The first three categories can be discussed in the core team. Individual care details remain restricted.

## 34.1 Cycle review agenda

| **Agenda** | **Review question** |
| --- | --- |
| **1. Outcomes** | Which principles and skills were actually understood and used? |
| **2. Timing** | Did Prem-kī-Kathā and discussion remain within structure? |
| **3. Participation** | Who dominated, disappeared, or lacked access? |
| **4. Practice** | Were saṅkalpas realistic? What common obstacles occurred? |
| **5. Family integration** | Did parent and child themes reinforce each other? |
| **6. Safety/boundaries** | Any confidentiality, mentor, correction, or escalation defects? |
| **7. Operations** | Arrival, room, transitions, materials, prasāda, childcare, and closing. |
| **8. Newcomers** | Did Setu and mentor support work without slowing the cohort? |
| **9. Change decisions** | Keep, correct, retire, or investigate. |
| **10. Capacity** | Can the next cycle proceed, or must enrollment/service be held? |

## 34.2 Pilot decision rules

- Continue when safety gates are intact, facilitators follow the method, and defects have owners.

- Continue with correction when problems are usable and bounded.

- Pause a lesson when its philosophy, case, or activity is unsafe or misleading.

- Pause mentor assignments after boundary breaches, conflicts of interest, or repeated overreach.

- Hold enrollment when facilitator, mentor, child-safety, space, or privacy capacity is exceeded.

- Escalate immediately when a reportable or urgent safety issue arises; do not wait for the cycle review.
