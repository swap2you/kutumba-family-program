# C1-W4 — Why Human Life Is Rare and Valuable

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| A family receives a valuable pass that can be used only once. The pass opens a learning center containing guidance, tools and a path to a lasting destination. But the family keeps it in a drawer. They are busy choosing snacks, comparing phones and planning someday to visit. When the expiry date approaches, they panic. The problem was not enjoying ordinary responsibilities; the problem was forgetting the pass’s unique purpose. |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Time snapshot: mark yesterday’s waking hours in broad categories without names or public sharing.

1. 5–12 min — Read the memory line from ŚB 11.9.29 and identify three ideas: rare, temporary, value-giving.

1. 12–18 min — Read a short summary of ŚB 7.6.1. Discuss formation in childhood without imposing adult standards.

1. 18–28 min — ‘Someday’ case study: work pressure, children’s activities, elder care and screens leave no protected spiritual time. Groups design a realistic fifteen-minute appointment.

1. 28–34 min — Question workshop: convert shallow or argumentative questions into inquiry that can guide practice.

1. 34–40 min — Calendar the family appointment and identify one likely interruption plus backup plan.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Gift box activity: inside are ears, voice, hands, heart and thinking symbols; children name how each can serve Kṛṣṇa.

- Story focus: young Prahlāda remembers and speaks about Kṛṣṇa even while young.

- Choice game: ‘now or later?’ for tiny devotional actions such as one prayer or helping set an altar cloth.

- Make a fifteen-minute family appointment ticket.

### Age 7-9

- Compare abilities of plants, animals and humans without disrespect: deliberate questions, moral choices and worship.

- Read a child-level paraphrase of ŚB 7.6.1 and identify what can begin in childhood.

- Time-jar experiment with large rocks, pebbles and sand: important things require intentional placement.

- Create one question for Kṛṣṇa, guru, sādhu or śāstra.

### Age 10-13

- Analyze ‘temporary’ versus ‘meaningless.’ Explain why a short opportunity can be highly valuable.

- Complete a private 24-hour attention audit and identify one low-value block.

- Discuss responsibility toward animals and the environment as souls within Kṛṣṇa’s creation.

- Design a realistic youth spiritual appointment that does not depend entirely on parental reminders.

## Questions: Discovery, Understanding and Application

### Discovery

- What three qualities of human life appear in ŚB 11.9.29?

- What does Prahlāda recommend beginning in childhood?

- Which ordinary human activities are also available in animal life?

### Understanding

- Why does temporary not mean worthless?

- What is the difference between urgency and anxiety?

- Why does greater human capacity create responsibility rather than superiority?

### Application

- Which fifteen minutes can your family protect this week?

- What spiritual question deserves continued study?

- What activity is consuming time without giving proportionate value?

## Bhakti Laboratory — How to Read One Verse as a Family

1. Display one verse reference and memory line; do not attempt the entire purport with young children.

1. Use the four-step method: hear/read, notice one word, explain in own words, choose one action.

1. Parents practice asking one discovery question and one application question without answering for the child.

1. Families complete a seven-minute trial reading using ŚB 11.9.29 or a child-appropriate summary.

## Teacher Preparation

- Read ŚB 11.9.29, 7.6.1 and 1.2.10; prepare age-appropriate paraphrases rather than graphic death emphasis.

- Prepare time-jar materials and private time-audit sheets.

- Ensure no teacher tells families to neglect work, school, health or elder-care obligations.

- Prepare examples for single-parent, shift-work and caregiving households.

- Brief teachers to respect animals and avoid derogatory species comparisons.
