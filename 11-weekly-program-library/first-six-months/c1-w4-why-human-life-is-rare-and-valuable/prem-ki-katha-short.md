## 6. Source-grounded narrative

_[Source narrative / paraphrase — not invented dialogue; compressed schedule]_

Before he became a teacher of the world, Nārada Muni passed through a forest where a hunter named Mṛgāri lived — one who half-killed animals so their cries would lure others. He thought pain was survival. When Nārada appeared, Mṛgāri prepared to attack. The scene shows how low human life can sink when mercy is forgotten — and how valuable it remains when reform is possible.

Nārada asked why Mṛgāri caused such suffering. The hunter answered honestly: his father had taught that half-violence brought benefit. He was **conditioned**, not a different species of person. Nārada's reply was simple: if half-killing creates bad karma, what of full killing? And if full killing is wrong, why half-kill at all? The hunter's logic collapsed. He saw habit as a chain, not a tradition. Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 11.9.29 teaches that human birth is rarely obtained, temporary, and able to give highest purpose when used rightly — Mṛgāri's story makes that verse visible.

Nārada did not leave him in guilt. He offered to provide for the household if Mṛgāri stopped the cruel work — **truth plus tangible help**. Mṛgāri agreed and broke his bow. Directed to worship Tulasī and chant, he walked sideways toward the cottage, trying not to harm ants. Outsiders laughed; Nārada said they could not see the inner transformation already begun (SB 6.1.58; 6.2.9).

Mṛgāri wept, asked how one so sinful could be delivered, and learned that sincere repentance and devotion attract Kṛṣṇa's mercy. The forest once known for violence became known for protection. Travelers sought his blessings. Bhagavad-gītā 7.19 describes surrender after many births; Mṛgāri compresses that arc into one life. Human life is valuable not for luxury but because **reform is possible** — even for a cruel hunter, even for a family turning one weekly hour toward hearing.

Prahlāda's instruction (SB 7.6.1) echoes: begin spiritual training early and gently. Mṛgāri was not a child, yet became teachable. SB 1.2.10 reminds that life's aim is self-realization. The turning point is the **broken bow** — a visible decision. Families may choose smaller signs: one cruel habit removed, one honest apology, one protected hour for hearing. Mṛgāri remained humble about his past; pride did not replace cruelty.

Youth ask whether people really change. Adults ask whether mercy to beings matters if the soul is eternal. This kathā answers: **mercy to living beings and mercy to the soul are not opponents**. Nārada stopped violence and opened devotion. By the end, the forest road recalls that human intelligence exists to serve Kṛṣṇa, not refine brutality.
