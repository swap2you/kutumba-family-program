# C2-W5 — Māyā: Decorating the Prison Cell

*Cycle 2 — Karma, Rebirth and Material Nature*

| **Field** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| **Week code** | C2-W5 |
| **Cycle function** | Case study and practical application |
| **Purpose** | Teach māyā as Kṛṣṇa’s difficult-to-overcome material energy that covers and misdirects the conditioned soul, while showing surrender, association and devotional practice as the way through—not hatred of the world or of women. |
| **Key verse** | Bhagavad-gītā 7.14 — daivī hy eṣā guṇa-mayī mama māyā duratyayā |
| **Memory line** | Kṛṣṇa’s material energy is difficult to overcome, but those who surrender to Him can cross beyond it. |

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| A person receives a notification while reading Bhagavad-gītā. ‘I will check for ten seconds,’ the mind says. Twenty minutes later, the person has moved through messages, news, shopping and comparison, and can barely remember the verse. Nothing forced the hand physically. Yet the sequence felt automatic. |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Attention snapshot: which item receives the first and last attention of the day? Private response.

1. 5–12 min — Read BG 7.14. Identify whose energy māyā is, what it consists of and how it is crossed.

1. 12–20 min — Map BG 2.62–63 as an attention-to-judgment chain using a non-sensitive example.

1. 20–28 min — Prison-cell case study: good householder responsibility versus making comfort, image or control the ultimate project.

1. 28–34 min — Explicit correction exercise: why ‘women are māyā’ is philosophically and ethically false.

1. 34–40 min — Design one boundary plus one shelter: device location, shopping delay, praise fast, japa buddy or mentor check-in.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Shiny-choice puppet: a flashing toy distracts from helping; children practice stop and choose.

- Sticky-note path: each repeated look adds a note until the path is hard to leave.

- Say clearly: ‘People are not māyā. Māyā is the energy that makes us forget Kṛṣṇa.’

- Redirect action: put down, take three breaths, chant once, choose help.

### Age 7-9

- Attention chain cards based on BG 2.62–63.

- Decorated-cage analogy: a beautiful cage is still not the bird’s freedom; discuss limits respectfully.

- Identify false promises in toy, game and popularity advertisements.

- Create one home device boundary with parents.

### Age 10-13

- Read BG 7.14 and map surrender as the exit rather than mere self-control.

- Analyze a recommendation algorithm as an engineered attention loop.

- Discuss how spiritual language can be misused to demean women or avoid responsibility.

- Create stop–name–redirect plans for one personal trap.

## Questions: Discovery, Understanding and Application

### Discovery

- How does BG 7.14 describe māyā?

- What sequence begins with contemplation in BG 2.62–63?

- What action is named as the way to cross māyā?

### Understanding

- Why is māyā not equal to ‘the world is fake’?

- When does responsible material arrangement become prison decoration?

- Why is calling women ‘māyā’ wrong?

### Application

- Which attention loop most often steals your intended devotional time?

- What false promise does it make?

- What boundary and devotional shelter will you add?

## Bhakti Laboratory — Attention Boundary and Japa Shelter

1. Place phones outside reach or use mock devices for the demonstration.

1. Write one false promise on paper and turn it face down.

1. Chant attentively for five minutes with a clear beginning and end.

1. After chanting, identify one practical boundary: charging location, app limit, no-device sacred space or delayed purchase.

1. Pair each boundary with shelter: replacement practice, family support or devotee accountability.

- Avoid public disclosure of addiction or compulsive behavior; offer private support pathways.
