## 6. Source-grounded narrative

_[Source narrative / paraphrase — not invented dialogue; compressed schedule]_

In the Fifth Canto of Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam, King Rahūgaṇa traveled with a royal palanquin and expected swift, respectful service. One bearer moved differently — careful not to step on ants or worms, adjusting his pace even when the king grew impatient. To Rahūgaṇa this looked like incompetence. Scripture presents it as the behavior of an advanced soul beneath a disguise.

That bearer was Jaḍa Bharata, who had passed through royal life, renunciation, and realization, yet now appeared as a simple worker. When the palanquin shook, the king rebuked the bearers harshly, assuming the usual hierarchy: the king is important; the carrier is merely a body that serves. Jaḍa Bharata answered without pride or violence. His reply was **spiritual instruction in plain language**: the king mistook the palanquin for the only reality. Carrier, king, and all on the road wear coverings; the living being who animates the body is distinct. Identifying the self with muscle, status, anger, or humiliation binds one in ignorance.

The scene is modest — a road, a palanquin, an irritated ruler — but answers large questions: "Who am I if my job changes?" and "Am I only my grades or reputation?" **Identity is not identical with role.** A king may be a bearer; a bearer may instruct a king. What continues is the conscious self.

Bhagavad-gītā 2.20 teaches the soul is not slain when the body changes; 2.17, that which pervades the body is indestructible; 13.23 distinguishes the individual soul and Supersoul; 15.7, the living being as an eternal fragmental part of Kṛṣṇa. Jaḍa Bharata makes those verses **visible**. Rahūgaṇa spoke from bodily identification — elevation meant superiority. Jaḍa Bharata's instruction exposed the error: both were souls in different roles. Neither insult nor praise touches the eternal self.

Families know how quickly we label by externals — accent, clothing, age, usefulness. Children learn identity from comparison. This kathā counters: worth is not exhausted by temporary role. Service to Kṛṣṇa can appear in any station when consciousness is purified. Jaḍa Bharata could have shamed the king; instead he spoke to liberate.

The turning point comes when Rahūgaṇa realizes he addressed a mahā-bhāgavata. Humiliation becomes repentance; the demand for external service becomes a request for internal instruction. **When pride breaks, hearing becomes possible.** Lāla–Lālī may remember the quiet carrier who protected little creatures. Kiśora–Kiśorī may ask who is treated as invisible because of role — reflection, not public confession.

Return to the road: a king confident in importance, a disguised saint protecting creatures the king never noticed. Between them passes this week's teaching: **the soul is unborn, eternal, and not destroyed when the body is destroyed.** The philosophy lesson will name jīva precisely; this kathā shows truth in a person on an ordinary path.
