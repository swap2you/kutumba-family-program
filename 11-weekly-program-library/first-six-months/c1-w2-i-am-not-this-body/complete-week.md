# C1-W2 — I Am Not This Body

*Cycle 1 — Identity and the Human Problem*

| **Field** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| **Week code** | C1-W2 |
| **Cycle function** | Foundational principle |
| **Purpose** | Help adults and children distinguish the changing body from the continuing conscious self, while learning to care for the body as Kṛṣṇa’s instrument rather than neglect or despise it. |
| **Key verse** | Bhagavad-gītā 2.13 — dehino ’smin yathā dehe |
| **Memory line** | The body changes from childhood to old age, but the conscious self continues. |
| **Bhakti laboratory** | Identity Pause and Japa Posture |
| **Minimum home practice** | Use the identity pause once daily at a chosen trigger. |

## Learning Outcomes

| **Outcome domain** | **By the end of the session, participants should be able to…** |
| --- | --- |
| **Knowledge** | Explain the body–self distinction using Bhagavad-gītā 2.13. |
| **Skill** | Use precise language: ‘I have a body’ rather than treating the body as the complete self. |
| **Practice** | Pause once daily and remember: ‘I am Kṛṣṇa’s eternal servant; this body is for service.’ |
| **Character** | Respect, sobriety, reduced vanity and reduced contempt toward self or others. |
| **Child** | Recognize that the same person remains through baby, child and older-body stages. |

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| A child finds an old family photo and laughs: ‘Dad, that cannot be you. You had so much hair!’ Everyone laughs, but the photo raises a serious question. The baby body is gone. The school-age body is gone. Even the cells and appearance have changed. Yet the person says, ‘That was me.’ Who is the ‘me’ that connects all those changing pictures? |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Photo timeline: silently note three major bodily changes and one sense of continuity.

1. 5–12 min — Read BG 2.13. Identify the changing elements, the continuing subject and the expected attitude of a sober person.

1. 12–20 min — Facilitator demonstration with three labeled jackets: child, adult, elder. One volunteer changes jackets while keeping the same name card.

1. 20–30 min — Case study: a teenager is mocked for appearance; a parent fears aging; another devotee neglects sleep saying, ‘I am not the body.’ Groups identify wrong conclusions and balanced responses.

1. 30–36 min — Language practice: replace five identity statements (‘I am ugly,’ ‘I am my job,’ ‘I am too old’) with truthful, non-denying statements.

1. 36–40 min — Choose one daily trigger for the identity pause.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Show baby, child and current photos of a volunteer with permission; children identify the same person.

- Costume relay: one child wears different safe costume pieces while holding the same name card.

- Care-for-the-instrument game: sort sleep, bathing, prasāda, hitting, exercise and unsafe behavior.

- Recall: ‘I have a body. I am a soul. I belong to Kṛṣṇa.’

### Age 7-9

- Build a three-stage body timeline and place one ‘same person’ thread through all stages.

- Discuss why ‘not the body’ does not mean ‘ignore the body.’

- Scenario cards on appearance, winning, injury and disability; choose a respectful response.

- Practice the first line of BG 2.13 with rhythm.

### Age 10-13

- Text observation of BG 2.13: what changes, what continues, what conclusion follows?

- Analyze social-media identity pressure and the difference between self-care and image obsession.

- Debate the false statement: ‘Because I am not the body, bodily harm and boundaries do not matter.’

- Write a one-sentence identity statement grounded in service.

## Questions: Discovery, Understanding and Application

### Discovery

- What bodily stages are named in BG 2.13?

- Who is the subject passing through those stages?

- What signs of continuity do you notice in your own life?

### Understanding

- Why does bodily change support the distinction between body and self?

- Why is neglecting health a misuse of this teaching?

- How does bodily identification intensify comparison?

### Application

- Which label most strongly controls your mood?

- How can your family speak about bodies without vanity or shame?

- What one act of body care will better support service this week?

## Bhakti Laboratory — Identity Pause and Japa Posture

1. Demonstrate stable, respectful sitting or walking posture without forcing children to remain still beyond capacity.

1. Guide three slow breaths; remind participants that breath and body are observed by the conscious self.

1. Chant one round section or five minutes together with attention to hearing.

1. Before and after, repeat: ‘I am not this changing body; I am Kṛṣṇa’s servant, using this body to hear and chant.’

## Teacher Preparation

- Read BG 2.13 and 13.1–3; avoid speculative claims about neuroscience or measuring the soul.

- Collect only consented photos or use generic age-stage images.

- Prepare safe costume pieces and scenario cards.

- Brief teachers not to invite discussion of body trauma, eating disorders or medical diagnoses in the group.

- Prepare an inclusive example for disability: the soul is not reduced by bodily ability, while support needs remain real.

## Assessment

- Adult explains BG 2.13 without saying the body is unreal or irrelevant.

- Adult gives one balanced response to a bodily-identity scenario.

- Child identifies the same person through changing age pictures.

- Teacher records misconceptions for private follow-up, not public correction battles.
