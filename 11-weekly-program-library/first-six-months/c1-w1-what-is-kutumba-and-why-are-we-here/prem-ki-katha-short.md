## 6. Source-grounded narrative

_[Source narrative / paraphrase — not invented dialogue; compressed schedule]_

Long after Lord Kṛṣṇa returned to His own abode, the earth felt Kali-yuga approaching. Human beings grew short-lived, distracted, and uncertain about life's purpose. In that setting, a great company of sages traveled to Naimiṣāraṇya on the bank of the Gomati River — not for comfort or fame, but because a serious question burned in their hearts.

Śaunaka Ṛṣi led the assembly. They lit the sacrificial fire, seated themselves with discipline, and asked the question of the age: **What should thoughtful persons do now that Kṛṣṇa has departed from ordinary sight?** Into that gathering came Sūta Gosvāmī, who had heard Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam from great authorities. The sages did not ask for political news or heavenly rewards. They asked what KUTUMBA families echo each week: **What is worth hearing and doing with this human life?** Sūta prepared to speak from paramparā, not imagination.

Naimiṣāraṇya is a place where serious persons withdraw from trivial talk to hear about the Lord. The sages understood that **time in Kali-yuga passes quickly** and that direction is needed before old age and death remove the chance to reform. Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 1.1.1 orients hearing toward the Absolute Truth as the source of all emanations. The remedy for the age is not a new invention. It is **hearing the Lord's glories in devotee association**. Verses this module holds central — 1.2.17 through 1.2.19 — teach that faithful hearing cleanses the heart, steadies devotion, and lifts the hearer toward goodness and happiness (1.2.18).

Picture the mood: hundreds of sages, quiet fire, river nearby, one speaker unfolding a literature meant for all peoples. No competing voices, no rushing away. The scene invites families: **Can we protect even one hour a week for this quality of hearing?** KUTUMBA is a disciplined family sādhana community — not a social club, substitute temple, or lecture series. It is a **protected weekly assembly** where parents and children turn toward authorized instruction together.

The narrative tension is **spiritual urgency**, not battlefield drama. The sages refuse cynicism and choose inquiry. Bhagavad-gītā 9.14 describes devotees who always chant and strive with determination; Caitanya-caritāmṛta Madhya 22.128 lists powerful bhakti practices including hearing. Children can see many respectful learners asking one question: "How should we live for Kṛṣṇa now?" Youth face a sharper challenge: **Will I spend attention on what disappears, or on what the sages traveled to hear?** Parents need not arrive as finished saints — only as **serious hearers** with protected time and faithful sources.

The Bhāgavatam's opening introduces a **culture of hearing** that KUTUMBA attempts in family form: curiosity without boasting, reverence without chaos, saṅga without isolation. By the end, the question becomes personal: not "What is KUTUMBA organizationally?" but "Why are **we** here this week?" The answer is gentle and demanding: to hear how to remember Kṛṣṇa faithfully in an age that pulls attention elsewhere. The resolution is **orientation** — weekly hearing as center, not decoration. Sūta is about to speak; the sages are ready; KUTUMBA families are invited into the same lineage — one verse, one practice, one sincere week at a time.
