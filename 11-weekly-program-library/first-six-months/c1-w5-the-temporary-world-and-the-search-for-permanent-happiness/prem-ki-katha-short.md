## 6. Source-grounded narrative

_[Source narrative / paraphrase — not invented dialogue; compressed schedule]_

Dhruva Mahārāja was only a boy when worldly honor proved unstable. He sought his father's lap; his stepmother said he had no right to the throne because he was not born in her womb. Affection was replaced by a lesson in material hierarchy. His mother Sunīti did not urge revenge. She told him gently: if he wanted a kingdom no one could remove, he should seek the Supreme Lord's shelter.

Dhruva left the palace — not rejecting family life, but rejecting the **illusion that bodily relation without Kṛṣṇa satisfies the heart**. In the forest, Nārada Muni tested whether the boy's pain had become devotion or ambition in spiritual dress. Nārada instructed him to meditate on the Lord's form and could have sent him home with moderate practice. Dhruva felt sharper urgency. He went deeper, controlled his senses, chanted the mantra given by Nārada, and at first still wanted a kingdom greater than his father's — **childlike intensity with mixed motive**, which scripture does not hide.

Months of austerity followed. The demigods marveled; the earth shook under his determination. His body grew thin; desire purified. Bhagavad-gītā 6.35 teaches the mind is controlled by practice; Dhruva embodied that as a child who refused to accept temporary insult as final truth.

The Lord appeared. Dhruva meant to complain, but seeing the Personality of Godhead's beauty and mercy, **he forgot the kingdom he had wanted** and could not speak his prepared prayers (SB 4.8–4.9). Kṛṣṇa touched his cheek with the conchshell; ignorance and mixture burned away. He offered wonder-filled prayers, remembered Sunīti and Nārada, and returned to duty without proud mysticism — an living example of BG 9.27: offer what you do to Kṛṣṇa.

Families feel restlessness despite comforts because temporary status cannot secure the heart. Children learn hurt can become prayer; teenagers see **temporary popularity cannot replace permanent relationship with Kṛṣṇa**; parents see good instruction redirects pain without denying it, as Sunīti did. BG 2.14 compares pleasure and pain to seasons; 8.15, the material world is temporary and full of distress; 5.22, sense pleasure mixes with misery; SB 1.2.10, material happiness cannot satisfy permanently.

The turning point is not the throne but the moment complaint dies before the Lord's beauty. Families may know smaller turns: one evening when kīrtana quiets complaining, one apology ending coldness, one protected prayer hour. Dhruva eventually ruled, but lasting happiness comes from **shelter at Kṛṣṇa's lotus feet**, not from securing every worldly throne.
