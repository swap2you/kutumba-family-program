# C1-W5 — The Temporary World and the Search for Permanent Happiness

*Cycle 1 — Identity and the Human Problem*

| **Field** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| **Week code** | C1-W5 |
| **Cycle function** | Case study, practice and worldview application |
| **Purpose** | Help families honestly recognize the limits of material arrangements, distinguish healthy enjoyment from the demand that temporary things provide permanent fulfillment, and redirect desire toward Kṛṣṇa-centered happiness. |
| **Key verse** | Bhagavad-gītā 8.15 — duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam |
| **Memory line** | This material world is temporary and cannot provide permanent freedom from distress; lasting fulfillment comes through attaining Kṛṣṇa. |
| **Bhakti laboratory** | Offering an Ordinary Activity to Kṛṣṇa |
| **Minimum home practice** | Before one enjoyable activity, pause for gratitude and three mahā-mantras. |

## Learning Outcomes

| **Outcome domain** | **By the end of the session, participants should be able to…** |
| --- | --- |
| **Knowledge** | Explain aśāśvatam as temporary and connect it with recurring change, separation and frustration. |
| **Skill** | Examine an expectation and identify whether a temporary object is being asked to provide permanent security or identity. |
| **Practice** | Offer one enjoyable family activity to Kṛṣṇa by adding remembrance, gratitude or service. |
| **Character** | Contentment, realism, gratitude and reduced entitlement. |
| **Child** | Recognize that toys, treats and winning can be enjoyable but do not stay forever. |

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| A family plans the perfect holiday. They choose the place, buy the clothes, arrange the food and imagine everyone smiling. Then the weather changes, a child becomes sick, the hotel room is noisy and two people argue. The holiday was not evil. The problem was the hidden demand: ‘This arrangement must make us completely happy.’ |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Private prompt: ‘A temporary arrangement I secretly expect to make me secure is…’ No forced sharing.

1. 5–12 min — Compare BG 2.14, 5.22 and 8.15. Identify temporary change, beginning/end and ultimate destination.

1. 12–20 min — Four-column exercise: pleasure, relief, meaning and spiritual nourishment. Classify examples; allow overlap but require reasons.

1. 20–30 min — Case studies: perfect family image, children’s achievement, consumption after stress and spiritualized entertainment. Identify the legitimate need and the misplaced demand.

1. 30–35 min — Connection practice using BG 9.27: choose an ordinary activity and add offering, gratitude, restraint or service.

1. 35–40 min — Prepare one sentence for a child disappointed by a canceled event without dismissing feelings.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Bubble activity: bubbles are beautiful and temporary; enjoy without crying when each one pops.

- Story: a child shares prasāda and discovers joy in giving, not only receiving.

- Sort pictures into ‘lasts a short time’ and ‘spiritual relationship grows through practice.’

- Gratitude prayer before a simple enjoyable activity.

### Age 7-9

- Build and gently dismantle a block tower; discuss change without calling the tower bad.

- Four-happiness cards: pleasure, relief, meaning and bhakti nourishment.

- Scenario: losing a game or breaking a favorite item; practice naming disappointment and choosing the next right action.

- Plan a Kṛṣṇa-centered family fun activity.

### Age 10-13

- Analyze advertising promises: what permanent need is attached to a temporary product?

- Discuss why detachment is not indifference and gratitude is not possession.

- Use BG 9.27 to redesign one entertainment, hobby or celebration.

- Create a ‘beginning–middle–end’ map for one desired experience.

## Questions: Discovery, Understanding and Application

### Discovery

- What words in the assigned verses describe temporary experience?

- What is the difference between an enjoyable object and an expectation placed upon it?

- What does BG 9.27 invite us to do with ordinary activities?

### Understanding

- Why is realism about suffering not pessimism?

- How can detachment increase care rather than reduce it?

- Why do repeated pleasures often fail to settle the mind permanently?

### Application

- Which family activity can be connected to Kṛṣṇa this week?

- What boundary would protect that activity from excess?

- How will you respond when the plan changes?

## Bhakti Laboratory — Offering an Ordinary Activity to Kṛṣṇa

1. Present four activity cards: meal, walk, music practice and celebration.

1. Families add four elements: remembrance before, devotional connection during, restraint/boundary and gratitude/service after.

1. Practice a simple gratitude prayer and one-minute kīrtana before a sample fruit snack.

1. Clarify that exact bhoga-offering procedures are taught only under approved worship guidance; this lab teaches intention and planning.

## Family Home Practice

| **Field** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| **Minimum** | Before one enjoyable activity, pause for gratitude and three mahā-mantras. |
| **Standard** | Add a boundary and one service element; discuss afterward what was enjoyable and what remained temporary. |
| **Stretch** | Complete a one-day ‘expectation audit’ whenever irritation arises: What did I demand from this person or object? |
| **Evidence** | One short family reflection; no need to report the cost or details publicly. |

## Teacher Preparation

- Read BG 2.14, 5.22, 8.15, 9.27 and SB 1.2.10 with purports.

- Prepare examples that do not shame income level, possessions, travel or family recreation.

- Prepare bubbles or block materials and ensure allergy-safe fruit if used.

- Brief teachers to validate disappointment before offering philosophy.

- Avoid making claims about a grieving family’s detachment or karma.

## Assessment

- Adult distinguishes temporary from evil and detachment from indifference.

- Family produces a four-part Kṛṣṇa-centered activity plan.

- Child names that disappointment is real but does not last forever.

- Facilitator checks for shame, denial or material hatred and corrects privately.
