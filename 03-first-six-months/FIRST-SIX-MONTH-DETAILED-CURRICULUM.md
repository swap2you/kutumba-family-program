# 1. How to Use This Curriculum

This volume converts the approved first-six-month architecture into complete active-week lesson packs. Each cycle contains five instructional/application weeks, one integration week and one protected off week. The off week has no KUTUMBA meeting, make-up class or assignment chase.

| **Core implementation rule** |
| --- |

# 2. Assumptions and Boundaries

- Founding cohort: approximately five to six families, generally 20–25 participants.

- Friday program: 6:15–9:00 p.m.; formal devotional session 6:30–9:00 p.m.

- Child bands: Bāla I ages 4–6, Bāla II ages 7–9, and Kiśora ages 10–13. Older teens may join the parent track or supervised service roles.

- Primary doctrinal authority: Śrīla Prabhupāda’s books and authorized ISKCON standards. Supplementary material requires review.

- Exact ārati, mantra, bhoga-offering and Deity-worship procedures are not independently invented in this curriculum.

- Assessment is private and non-competitive. No public rankings, awards for spiritual performance or family comparisons.

- KUTUMBA supports temple participation, progressive sādhana and study readiness; it does not replace temple authority, initiation procedures or accredited Bhakti Śāstrī study.

# 3. Standard Friday Architecture

| **Time** | **Function** |
| --- | --- |
| **6:15–6:30** | Arrival, attendance, bhoga handoff, setup, children settled, phones silenced |
| **6:30–7:00** | Kīrtana and worship according to approved standards |
| **7:00–7:20** | Prem-kī-Kathā: family problem, scripture, principle, analogy and application |
| **7:20–8:00** | Parallel parent and age-banded child formation |
| **8:00–8:20** | Bhakti laboratory: practical devotional skill |
| **8:20–8:30** | Family saṅkalpa: one achievable home commitment |
| **8:30–9:00** | Prasāda, supervised serving and formal close |

# 4. Age-Band Delivery Model

**4-6:** Bāla I (ages 4–6): concrete, sensory, movement-based, one principle and one action.

**7-9:** Bāla II (ages 7–9): story, sequence, comparison, short verse phrase and guided application.

**10-13:** Kiśora (ages 10–13): evidence, scenario analysis, short text work, discussion and service responsibility.

Mixed-age rule: teach one common spiritual principle, then vary vocabulary, duration, abstraction, reading load, movement and responsibility. Never make an older child the sole supervisor of younger children.

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| Friday evening has finally arrived. One parent is tired from work, another is worried about unfinished chores, one child wants a tablet, and another asks whether KUTUMBA is only another class. This is the real starting point: a family does not become Kṛṣṇa conscious because everyone already feels inspired. A family grows because, despite ordinary pressure, it repeatedly creates a protected place for hearing, chanting, service and honest improvement. |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Silent arrival question: ‘What do I want my home to feel like spiritually one year from now?’ Each adult writes privately.

1. 5–10 min — Read the memory line from ŚB 1.2.18. Facilitator explains book Bhāgavata, devotee Bhāgavata, regularity and service without giving a long purport lecture.

1. 10–18 min — Six-purpose card sort. Families match the charter purposes with examples: home chanting, temple service, respectful correction, festival participation, study readiness and family cooperation.

1. 18–28 min — Case study: a family attends every Friday but has no home practice; another practices at home but withdraws from temple association. Discuss what each is missing and how to rebalance without criticism.

1. 28–35 min — Family operating agreement: each couple or adult household identifies one support they need and one contribution they can reliably make.

1. 35–40 min — Draft the first saṅkalpa using the formula: specific action + frequency + trigger + minimum version.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Story focus: young Nārada helps saintly visitors and listens carefully.

- Movement game: four corners labeled Hear, Chant, Serve and Respect. Children move to the corner named in a short scenario.

- Craft: family garden sheet; place one paper seed for each devotional action the family wants to grow.

- Recall phrase: ‘We help each other remember Kṛṣṇa.’

### Age 7-9

- Sequence cards: Nārada serves, hears, honors prasāda, remembers and grows.

- Team task: sort activities into ‘helps our bhakti garden,’ ‘can be offered to Kṛṣṇa,’ and ‘needs a boundary.’

- Create a one-week family practice bookmark with one action and two check boxes.

- Verse phrase: nityaṁ bhāgavata-sevayā.

### Age 10-13

- Read a short facilitator-prepared excerpt summary of ŚB 1.2.18 and identify the repeated actions and promised result.

- Discuss the difference between attendance, belonging and actual practice.

- Analyze two mini-cases: spiritual competition and passive spectatorship.

- Choose one youth service that is helpful but does not make the youth responsible for adult safety or discipline.

## Prem-kī-Kathā Script

*Delivery target: 8–10 minutes of scripted narration plus 5–7 minutes of interaction. Do not exceed the 20-minute block.*

| A child finds an old family photo and laughs: ‘Dad, that cannot be you. You had so much hair!’ Everyone laughs, but the photo raises a serious question. The baby body is gone. The school-age body is gone. Even the cells and appearance have changed. Yet the person says, ‘That was me.’ Who is the ‘me’ that connects all those changing pictures? |
| --- |

## Parent Lesson — 40 Minutes

1. 0–5 min — Photo timeline: silently note three major bodily changes and one sense of continuity.

1. 5–12 min — Read BG 2.13. Identify the changing elements, the continuing subject and the expected attitude of a sober person.

1. 12–20 min — Facilitator demonstration with three labeled jackets: child, adult, elder. One volunteer changes jackets while keeping the same name card.

1. 20–30 min — Case study: a teenager is mocked for appearance; a parent fears aging; another devotee neglects sleep saying, ‘I am not the body.’ Groups identify wrong conclusions and balanced responses.

1. 30–36 min — Language practice: replace five identity statements (‘I am ugly,’ ‘I am my job,’ ‘I am too old’) with truthful, non-denying statements.

1. 36–40 min — Choose one daily trigger for the identity pause.

## Children’s Parallel Lesson — 40 Minutes

*Common child timing: 0–5 opening/recall; 5–15 story or text; 15–28 movement, art, game or investigation; 28–35 verse/recall; 35–40 cleanup and family transition.*

### Age 4-6

- Show baby, child and current photos of a volunteer with permission; children identify the same person.

- Costume relay: one child wears different safe costume pieces while holding the same name card.

- Care-for-the-instrument game: sort sleep, bathing, prasāda, hitting, exercise and unsafe behavior.

- Recall: ‘I have a body. I am a soul. I belong to Kṛṣṇa.’

### Age 7-9

- Build a three-stage body timeline and place one ‘same person’ thread through all stages.

- Discuss why ‘not the body’ does not mean ‘ignore the body.’

- Scenario cards on appearance, winning, injury and disability; choose a respectful response.

- Practice the first line of BG 2.13 with rhythm.

### Age 10-13

- Text observation of BG 2.13: what changes, what continues, what conclusion follows?

- Analyze social-media identity pressure and the difference between self-care and image obsession.

- Debate the false statement: ‘Because I am not the body, bodily harm and boundaries do not matter.’

- Write a one-sentence identity statement grounded in service.

# Appendix B — Universal Facilitation Rules

- No uninterrupted adult teaching segment longer than ten minutes without a question, text observation, pair task or case.

- Ask discovery questions before giving interpretation; ask application questions only after the principle is clear.

- Never demand emotional disclosure. Participants may pass, write privately or use a fictional case.

- Correct philosophy calmly and specifically. Do not shame a participant or child in front of the group.

- Do not diagnose karma, spiritual level, family problems, mental illness or guru qualification.

- Use “I do not know; I will verify” whenever the source or policy is uncertain.

- Children remain under designated trained adult supervision in visible spaces; parents remain responsible for their children.

- Outings, festival programs and integration nights replace the normal weekly program rather than adding another burden.

# Appendix E — Master Sensitive-Topic Guardrails

- Body and soul: never dismiss health, disability, abuse, emotions or bodily safety.

- Human life: avoid death anxiety, species contempt and adult standards imposed on children.

- Temporary world: do not trivialize grief, poverty, illness or family affection.

- Karma: never identify a specific cause for another person’s suffering; reject victim-blaming.

- Free will: do not imply victims chose harm or that conditioning removes all responsibility.

- Reincarnation: no predictions, ghost stories or fear-based control.

- Modes: never label people, genders, cultures, diagnoses or families as a mode.

- Māyā: women are not māyā; material responsibility is not condemned.

- God and Kṛṣṇa: teach clear siddhānta without mocking other traditions or devas.

- Guru: no pressure toward a guru or initiation; respect never overrides safeguarding and accountability.

- Holy name: no performance ranking or imitation of advanced symptoms.

- Bhakti processes: no checklist competition and no unapproved worship procedure.
