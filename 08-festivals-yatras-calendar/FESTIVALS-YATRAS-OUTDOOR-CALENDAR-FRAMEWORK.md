# Purpose of this document

This document converts the KUTUMBA master operating model into a practical experiential-calendar system. It governs festivals, pilgrimages, cow and temple service, nature activities, informal gatherings, travel, safety, budgeting and post-event learning for a small US-based family cohort.

The design objective is not to maximize the number of events. It is to create a limited number of memorable experiences that deepen hearing, chanting, service, family bonds and meaningful connection with temple life without producing calendar overload.

## How to use it

**1.  **Adopt Sections 1–3 as permanent program policy.

**2.  **Build the next year’s calendar using the annual workflow in Section 4.

**3.  **Use the festival and venue playbooks to design each selected experience.

**4.  **Complete the event brief, risk review, permissions and emergency plan from the appendices.

**5.  **After the event, capture learning and operational defects so the next version improves.

## Key assumptions

**•  ****Cohort size: **approximately five to six families and 20–25 participants.

**•  ****Geographic base: **Central Pennsylvania, with regional day trips and occasional travel to nearby ISKCON communities.

**•  ****Annual rhythm: **40 active KUTUMBA weeks and 12 protected non-program weeks.

**•  ****Child responsibility: **parents remain responsible unless a child is formally checked into a designated KUTUMBA activity.

**•  ****Calendar authority: **festival dates, fasting and pāraṇa are taken from the locally applicable authorized ISKCON calendar and temple guidance, not hard-coded in this document.

# 1.1 Experiential formation purpose

Festivals and yātrās are not rewards added after “real learning.” They are part of the learning system. Children and adults remember what they hear more deeply when they also sing, walk, serve, observe, present, travel, honor prasāda and reflect together.

The master operating model already places utsava and yātrā among KUTUMBA’s core methods and requires that every picnic, trek, farm visit or informal gathering retain a real spiritual centre. The same model fixes the no-overloading rule: one principal program per week, protected breaks and no competition in service or participation [S01].

## 1.2 Six experiential outcomes

| **Outcome** | **What success looks like** |
| --- | --- |
| **Joy** | Families associate devotional life with warmth, beauty, service and meaningful shared experiences. |
| **Belonging** | Participants know one another through service and reliability, not gossip, status or private factions. |
| **Devotional memory** | Children and adults can recall a person, pastime, teaching, place or service after the event. |
| **Practice** | The event results in at least one realistic home or temple practice. |
| **Temple connection** | KUTUMBA participation increases respect for and service to temple life rather than replacing it. |
| **Character** | Punctuality, cleanliness, humility, cooperation, responsibility and care for living beings improve. |

## 1.3 Five devotional anchors

*Figure 1. Five anchors that keep an activity Kṛṣṇa-centred.*

Every activity uses these anchors proportionately. A short park meeting may need a five-minute prayer, a fifteen-minute story and a closing reflection. A full yātrā needs a stronger sādhana, hearing and service structure. Token devotional branding is not enough.

## 1.4 Non-negotiable boundaries

**•  **No event is added merely because it is attractive, popular or available.

**•  **No activity becomes a competition in cooking, costume, singing, knowledge, travel spending or children’s performance.

**•  **No independent worship procedure, fasting rule, Deity practice or festival ritual is invented by KUTUMBA.

**•  **No child is forced to stay late, fast, perform, hike beyond capacity or join a frightening dramatization.

**•  **No temple, farm or venue is treated as a consumer attraction; the host’s devotional and operational authority is respected.

**•  **No expensive event is presented as a test of faith or belonging.

**•  **No protected rest week is casually converted into another KUTUMBA obligation.

**•  **No confidential family, child, medical or transport information is stored in Git or published in group channels.

## 1.5 Scriptural and institutional basis

**•  **Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 7.14 connects household life with repeated saintly association, hearing, prasāda distribution, care for living beings and visiting holy places [S03].

**•  **Caitanya-caritāmṛta, Madhya 13 describes Ratha-yātrā through kīrtana, dancing, service and the mercy of Lord Jagannātha [S04].

**•  **The GBC adopted a multi-calendar approach because observances must be calculated for local conditions rather than imposed from one global time zone [S02].

**•  **KUTUMBA therefore teaches the meaning and conduct of observances while relying on the local temple and authorized calendar for dates and exact practice.

# 3.1 Calendar authority and annual refresh

The annual calendar must be generated from the locally applicable Vaiṣṇava calendar and the designated temple’s published schedule. The GBC’s multi-calendar policy recognizes that location matters [S02]. Do not copy dates from an Indian calendar, a social-media post or the previous year.

**1.  **By 15 October, obtain the authorized calendar source and the local temple’s provisional festival schedule.

**2.  **Mark school breaks, family travel periods, major temple events and severe-weather buffers.

**3.  **Place the six curriculum-cycle breaks first and protect them.

**4.  **Select only four replacement experiences using the portfolio rules.

**5.  **Map integrated festival Fridays to the relevant curriculum theme.

**6.  **Review clashes, costs, travel time, host capacity and child burden.

**7.  **Temple Liaison verifies public-festival and service assumptions.

**8.  **Children and Safety Lead reviews every off-site or late-night item.

**9.  **Publish the annual calendar by 15 December with a “dates subject to authorized update” note.

**10.  **Reconfirm each event quarterly and again at its formal planning gates.

## 3.3 Selection scorecard for replacement events

| **Criterion** | **Weight** | **Question** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Devotional value** | 25% | Will participants hear, chant, serve, remember and connect to authorized teaching? |
| **Cohort accessibility** | 20% | Can young children, older adults, busy parents and varied incomes participate? |
| **Temple/host relationship** | 15% | Does it strengthen appropriate temple or community service? |
| **Safety and control** | 20% | Can KUTUMBA reliably manage transport, weather, supervision and emergency response? |
| **Cost and burden** | 10% | Is the financial and time cost proportionate to the value? |

## 3.4 Change-control rules

**•  **A date change does not automatically preserve the event; rerun transport, cost, host and weather checks.

**•  **A new event can replace an approved slot only through the Program Director and Safety Lead.

**•  **A cancelled event usually becomes a rest week or home guide; it is not automatically rescheduled.

**•  **A festival date correction is communicated as an authorized calendar update, not treated as an error by families.

**•  **The published calendar shows mandatory KUTUMBA events, optional temple observances and protected weeks distinctly.

## 4.9 Śrīla Prabhupāda appearance and disappearance observances

These observances should cultivate gratitude, careful hearing and commitment to Śrīla Prabhupāda’s mission. They must not become polished performance events in which families compete to offer elaborate speeches or gifts.

| **Component** | **Working standard** |
| --- | --- |
| **Source** | Approved biography, recorded lecture, letter or direct passage from Śrīla Prabhupāda’s books. |
| **Family offering** | One sincere gratitude, one instruction being applied or one service commitment. |

| **Boundary** | Do not invent biographical stories or circulate unverified quotations. |

## 7.4 Nature observation

Nature activities should avoid vague spirituality. The facilitator selects one authorized theological or ethical connection—dependence on Kṛṣṇa, care for living beings, the modes of nature, gratitude, simplicity or seeing the Lord’s energies—then uses direct observation to reinforce it.

| **Prompt type** | **Example** |
| --- | --- |
| **Observation** | Find three forms of dependence: water, shelter, food, light, soil or cooperation. |
| **Scriptural connection** | Read a brief assigned verse or passage before the walk. |
| **Service** | Collect litter where permitted; leave plants and habitats undisturbed. |
| **Reflection** | What did this place help us remember about Kṛṣṇa or our responsibility? |

## 7.5 Trekking and hiking

Choose routes by the least-capable expected participant, not by the enthusiasm of the strongest adult. The National Park Service advises hikers to know their limits, plan ahead and choose difficulty according to actual experience and conditions [S21–S22].

| ☐  Distance, elevation, surface and expected duration published. | ☐  Restrooms, shade, water, stroller suitability and turnaround point identified. |
| --- | --- |
| ☐  Route downloaded or carried offline; trailhead and emergency access recorded. | ☐  Front and sweep adults assigned; no participant is pressured to keep the fast group’s pace. |
| ☐  Headcount at trailhead, route changes, turnaround and return. | ☐  Weather, heat, lightning and daylight window checked. |
| ☐  Sturdy footwear, water, layers, sun/insect protection and essential medication required. | ☐  Alternative short route or stationary activity available when feasible. |

## 7.6 Ice-cream or informal family gathering

**•  **Limit to one or two per year and 60–75 minutes.

**•  **Use a fixed start, one brief devotional remembrance and a clear closing time.

**•  **Parents supervise their children; the gathering does not spread into uncontrolled shopping or roaming.

**•  **Review obvious dietary conflicts, but do not represent commercial food as prasāda unless properly offered through the approved process.

**•  **No family is criticized for declining, leaving early or choosing a different permitted item.

**•  **Do not use informal gatherings for confidential care discussions, gossip or temple politics.

## 7.7 Kayaking — later-stage high-risk activity

|  | **Year 1 decision** |
| --- | --- |

**•  **Use an established rental/operator on calm, sheltered water; no volunteer-run improvised expedition.

**•  **Require parent participation for minors and prohibit unsupervised child boats.

**•  **Require a properly fitted USCG-approved life jacket for every participant for the entire activity—stronger than the Pennsylvania minimum.

**•  **Pennsylvania requires children 12 and younger to wear life jackets while underway in canoes, kayaks and paddleboards; each paddler must have an approved, correctly fitted device [S18].

**•  **File a float plan; check weather and water conditions; cancel for thunder, strong wind, cold water, high flow or operator concern [S19].

**•  **No swimming is folded into the KUTUMBA activity.

**•  **Use event-specific consent and confirm medication/waterproof storage needs.

## 7.8 Overnight camping

Camping is not recommended before Year 3. It combines lodging, fire, cooking, wildlife, darkness, bathrooms, weather and child-safeguarding risk. A temple or established campground with formal facilities is preferable to remote camping.

**•  **Children sleep with their own family; no mixed unrelated adult-child sleeping arrangements.

**•  **No open flame or stove use by children; designated trained adults control fire and fuel.

**•  **Separate night supervision, bathroom and lost-child procedures are documented.

**•  **Quiet hours and morning sādhana are realistic for families, not punitive.

**•  **A severe-weather shelter and evacuation plan is mandatory.

**•  **Retain receipts and reconcile actual versus approved cost within seven days.

**•  **Do not reimburse unapproved upgrades, souvenirs or personal purchases.

**•  **Material cost increase requires a new family decision, not silent escalation.

**•  **Unused subsidy or donation is handled under the approved financial policy and not casually retained by an individual.

## 15.3 Success measures

| **Domain** | **Evidence** | **Bad metric to avoid** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Devotional memory** | Families can explain one teaching, person, place or service after 1–4 weeks. | Number of photos or social-media reactions. |
| **Practice** | At least one realistic home/temple action adopted. | Publicly displayed commitments or round counts. |
| **Belonging** | Families report being welcomed and useful. | Popularity of hosts or performers. |
| **Temple connection** | More respectful service and participation. | KUTUMBA attendance substituting for temple life. |
| **Safety** | No preventable incident; near misses produce corrective action. | Claiming success because “nothing happened.” |
| **Burden** | Families can participate without chronic exhaustion or cost pressure. | Maximizing events or attendance at every festival. |
