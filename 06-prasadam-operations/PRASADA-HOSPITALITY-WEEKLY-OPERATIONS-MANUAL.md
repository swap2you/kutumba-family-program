# 1. Purpose, scope and operating assumptions

KUTUMBA’s prasāda system exists to support the Friday program, not dominate it. Its purpose is to make clean, appropriately offered, safely handled and hospitably served food available through a sustainable family-service rotation.

## 1.1 Scope

• Five to six participating families, normally 20–25 people including children.

• Friday gatherings with a formal close around 9:00 p.m.

• Food prepared primarily in participating family kitchens and transported to the venue.

• One primary cook family, one operations backup family and distributed non-cooking roles.

• Private cohort operations. Any public sale, open public event, fundraiser or major scale-up requires a separate regulatory and risk review.

## 1.2 Key assumptions

| **Assumption** | **Launch implication** |
| --- | --- |
| The venue permits outside home-prepared food | Obtain written or documented confirmation before launch. |
| Electrical warming equipment is permitted | Verify circuits, fire rules, equipment placement and extension-cord policy. |
| Families can transport food under temperature control | Purchase insulated carriers and verify temperatures at departure and arrival. |
| At least one adult per family can serve | Collect availability and limitations during orientation. |
| Exact worship process will be approved separately | Do not use this manual to invent mantras, altar procedures or qualifications. |

## 1.3 What this system deliberately avoids

• Forty different menus, elaborate weekly feasts or personal-menu improvisation.

• One highly capable family becoming the permanent cook or rescuer.

• Using devotional language to avoid hard food-safety decisions.

• Public comparison of cooking, cost, children, hosting or service quality.

• An unofficial treasury through a personal payment account.

• Assuming a nonprofit or private gathering is automatically exempt from venue, licensing or inspection requirements.

# 2. Operating doctrine

## 2.1 Twelve non-negotiables

• Prasāda is simple: three principal preparations are enough.

• There is no cooking competition and no unsolicited extra dish.

• There is no public criticism of another family’s offering.

• Food-safety correction is required and is not treated as criticism.

• The primary cook family does not lead cleanup that week.

• The kīrtana family remains with its assigned devotional service.

• Food arrives cooked and ready for authorised offering and controlled holding.

• Nobody tastes, samples or eats the food before the approved offering process is complete.

• Unknown ingredients, unclear preparation status or unsafe temperatures stop service.

• Children do not handle hot vessels, electrical warmers or food-safety decisions.

• No family performs an independent role before observation, shadowing and sign-off.

• Respect for prasāda never requires serving food that may be unsafe.

## 2.2 No criticism does not mean no control

| **Prohibited public commentary** | **Required private operational review** |
| --- | --- |
| “Their food was too spicy.” | Was the controlled recipe followed? |
| “We cooked much more.” | Was the portion tier correct and what was the actual leftover? |
| “Their children did not eat.” | Was the menu age-appropriate and was serving delayed? |
| “They used cheap ingredients.” | Were ingredients approved, safe and within the recipe specification? |
| “Our family hosts better.” | Did the station checklist pass? |

|  | **Rule  **Taste preference is not an incident. Unknown ingredients, allergen errors, unsafe temperature, contamination or recurring shortage are incidents and must be documented. |
| --- | --- |

## 2.3 Source-authority ladder

When requirements conflict, use this order:

| **Priority** | **Authority** | **Examples** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Applicable law, regulator and venue | Food rules, electrical restrictions, fire code, insurance, kitchen access |
| 2 | Qualified ISKCON or local worship authority | Bhoga preparation and handoff, authorised offering, Ekādaśī and festival standards |
| 3 | Participant medical plan | Allergy emergency plan, medication instructions, dietary medical restrictions |
| 4 | KUTUMBA controlled manual | Roster, menu, communication, checklists and records |
| 5 | Personal custom or preference | Family recipe habits, preferred spice level, preferred serving style |

# 3. Governance, roles and stop authority

## 3.1 Core accountability

| **Role** | **Owns** | **May stop or change** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Operations and Prasāda Lead | Roster, menus, recipes, temperature controls, equipment, supplies, contingency and cost review | May isolate or reject food; may simplify or cancel meal service. |
| Kīrtana and Worship Lead | Approved songs, altar readiness, bhoga handoff and exact offering SOP | May stop an unauthorised or unclear offering process. |
| Children and Safety Lead | Child positioning, allergy plans, emergency response and incident records | May stop service or an activity for child or allergy safety. |
| Documentation and Calendar Lead | Master calendar, reminders, controlled forms, versioning and published materials | May reject private verbal swaps until the roster is updated. |
| Temple Liaison / Venue Contact | Temple alignment, venue permissions, regulator or insurance clarification | May pause launch until approval gaps are closed. |
| Weekly Station Lead | Completes the station checklist and coordinates helpers | Escalates; does not silently improvise policy. |

## 3.2 Independent stop authority

|  | **Food safety  **The Operations Lead stops food movement or service when ingredients, temperature, contamination or illness controls are unresolved. |
| --- | --- |

|  | **Worship integrity  **The Worship Lead stops the offering process when the authorised person, utensils, sequence or status is unclear. |
| --- | --- |

|  | **Child and allergy safety  **The Safety Lead stops any activity or meal service that conflicts with a child’s emergency plan or creates an uncontrolled hazard. |
| --- | --- |

The three authorities consult one another, but no one must wait for group consensus to stop an immediate risk.

## 3.3 Decision matrix

| **Situation** | **Immediate owner** | **Decision** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary cook cancels Tuesday | Operations | Activate backup or reassign by 9:30 p.m. |
| Dish includes an undeclared packaged ingredient | Operations | Hold item; verify label; reject if unresolved. |
| Offering person unavailable | Worship | Use approved alternate or modify/cancel offering according to local SOP. |
| Child with severe allergy has no medication/action plan | Safety | No communal meal participation until a safe arrangement is documented. |
| Venue disallows home-prepared TCS food | Venue/Temple Liaison | Do not work around the rule; redesign the meal model. |
| Power circuit fails | Operations | Move to validated equipment/circuit; otherwise apply time controls or stop service. |
| Public fundraiser proposed | Governance + Liaison | Run separate legal, financial, insurance and food-service review. |

# 4. Venue, legal and insurance verification

The program must not assume that a private, nonprofit or religious gathering is exempt from all food requirements. Pennsylvania distinguishes licensing from broader compliance; an exempt facility may still be subject to inspection and other food-safety provisions. Local jurisdictions and venue policies can be stricter.

## 4.1 Pre-launch venue questions

**☐ **Are families allowed to bring home-prepared hot and cold food?

**☐ **Is the gathering private, invitation-only, open to the public, or connected to a sale or donation?

**☐ **Which agency has jurisdiction: Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture, county or local health department?

**☐ **Does the venue require a food permit, certified food manager, insurance endorsement or waiver?

**☐ **Which kitchens, sinks, refrigerators, outlets, circuits, warmers and cleaning chemicals may be used?

**☐ **Are open flames, Sterno or daisy-chained extension cords prohibited?

**☐ **Who owns incident reporting and emergency contact with the venue?

**☐ **How must waste, grease, wastewater and leftovers be handled?

|  | **Recommendation  **Obtain a one-page written venue operating approval. Oral assumptions are weak control and will fail when leadership or venue staff changes. |
| --- | --- |

## 4.2 Trigger for a separate event plan

A separate plan is mandatory when any of these occurs: food is sold; donations are tied to food; the event is publicly advertised; attendance exceeds the controlled cohort; food vendors are used; food is prepared on site at scale; or the program operates at a fair, festival or temporary venue.

# 5. Six-family rotating roster

Figure 1. Six independent service stations prevent role confusion and permanent dependency.

## 5.1 Service stations

| **Code** | **Station** | **Controlled responsibility** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| P | Prasāda Primary | Prepare, label and transport the approved three-item meal. Complete departure temperature checks. |
| O | Operations Backup | Prepare venue, warmers and temperature logs; retain emergency-menu capacity; manage contingencies. |
| B | Bhoga and Worship Support | Receive compliant bhoga, prepare approved utensils and support the authorised offering process. |
| K | Kīrtana and Ārati | Lead approved kīrtana and prepare instruments and worship materials; no kitchen diversion. |
| W | Welcome and Children | Attendance, guest orientation, children’s transition and lesson-material readiness. |
| S | Serving and Cleanup | Set serving line, supervise utensils, coordinate dishes, waste, floors and final reset. |

## 5.2 Six-week rotation

| **Active week** | **Family 1** | **Family 2** | **Family 3** | **Family 4** | **Family 5** | **Family 6** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Week 1 | P | O | B | K | W | S |
| Week 2 | O | B | K | W | S | P |
| Week 3 | B | K | W | S | P | O |
| Week 4 | K | W | S | P | O | B |
| Week 5 | W | S | P | O | B | K |
| Week 6 | S | P | O | B | K | W |

## 5.3 Rotation rules

• A family may swap roles, but a swap is valid only after the Calendar Lead updates the master roster.

• Routine swaps close Thursday at 8:00 p.m.; emergencies are escalated immediately.

• A missed service is rescheduled fairly and privately, not used for public correction.

• Repeated unreliability is handled under family care and governance, not through gossip.

• The cooking family is exempt from leading cleanup; it may voluntarily help only after its cooking burden is complete.

• Every station lead coordinates helpers but remains accountable for checklist completion.

# 6. Five-family fallback roster

## 6.1 When the backup must become the cook

• OC becomes Primary Cook and uses Emergency Menu E1 or E2.

• BS becomes Cleanup Lead.

• W adds Serving Lead after welcome and child transition.

• Optional dishes are cancelled.

• All non-cooking adults participate in teardown.

• The emergency arrangement is recorded; it must not become the standard pattern.

# 7. First-month training and shadow service

Figure 2. No independent service before demonstration, rehearsal, shadowing and competency release.

## 7.1 Weeks 1–4: demonstration and rehearsal

| **Week** | **Training objective** | **Required practice** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 — Complete demonstration | See the whole system | Core team runs arrival, bhoga handoff, holding, serving and teardown; families observe. |
| 2 — Station walk-through | Understand role boundaries | Six short demonstrations: recipe card, transport, temperature, offering boundary, welcome and cleanup. |
| 3 — Safety and failure drills | Practise stopping, not improvising | Primary cancellation, unknown ingredient, low arrival temperature, allergy alert and power failure. |
| 4 — Full rehearsal | Operate under direct supervision | Families perform assigned stations; trainers sign only observed competencies. |

## 7.2 Weeks 5–8: shadow service

The assigned family performs the station while a trained core-team member remains accountable. The trainer observes, corrects only when necessary and records objective evidence. A shadow week is not automatic approval.

## 7.3 Competency release criteria

| **Role** | **Minimum evidence before independent assignment** |
| --- | --- |
| Primary Cook | Follows recipe version; checks packaged ingredients; avoids tasting; labels containers; records departure temperature; arrives on time; escalates changes. |
| Operations Backup | Sets safe equipment; sanitises probe thermometer; records four temperatures; knows emergency menus; can apply corrective decision tree. |
| Bhoga Support | Maintains offered/unoffered separation; protects worship utensils; follows the authorised handoff; does not invent procedure. |
| Kīrtana and Ārati | Uses approved material; sets equipment safely; remains in assigned role; returns assets. |
| Welcome and Children | Completes attendance; welcomes guests; protects private allergy information; manages child transition and seating. |
| Serving and Cleanup | Controls utensils; keeps children from heat; applies leftover labels; completes wash-rinse-sanitise-air-dry and final sign-off. |

## 7.4 Training records

Each competency is marked Observed, Practised, Demonstrated or Released. “Attended training” alone does not prove operational competence.

# 8. Menu architecture

## 8.1 Standard three-item pattern

| **Component** | **Normal selection** | **Boundary** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Grain | Rice, pulao, lemon rice or khichadi base | One only. |
| Dal / protein preparation | Moong, toor or chana dal; khichadi may combine grain and dal | One only. |
| Vegetable | One mild seasonal sabji | One only. |
| Optional | Whole fruit, simple salad, approved raita or one festival sweet | Scheduled, not spontaneous. |

|  | **No menu inflation  **An optional item does not authorise five extra preparations. Festival joy is not measured by dish count. |

## 8.3 Why eight menus, not forty

• Families learn stable recipes rather than experimenting under pressure.

• Ingredient labels and allergen declarations become predictable.

• Temperature and transport performance can be calibrated.

• Cost and leftover data become comparable.

• Backup families can realistically stock emergency ingredients.

• Children gain familiarity without the menu becoming monotonous.

## 8.4 Ekādaśī and festival override

|  | **Worship approval required  **The ordinary grain-and-dal menu must not run automatically on Ekādaśī or a fasting/festival observance. Practices can differ by Deity, participant and local authority. The Worship Lead publishes the approved menu and participant guidance at least one week ahead. |
| --- | --- |

Do not create a permanent “Ekādaśī recipe rule” in this operations manual without qualified local review. Food-safety controls still apply to dairy, potatoes, fruit, nuts, refrigeration and cross-contact.

| Child age 6–12 | 0.60 |
| Child age 2–5 | 0.35 |
| Child under 2 | Parent-managed; use 0.15 only when a shared portion is expected |

## 9.2 Portion tiers

| **Tier** | **Planned portions** | **Use** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | 20 | Low-attendance controlled week |
| B | 25 | Default founding-cohort tier |
| C | 30 | Approved guests or higher attendance; not an invitation to exceed venue capacity |

| Dry dal | 0.88 kg | 1.10 kg | 1.32 kg |

|  | **Pilot status  **These are version 0.9 starting quantities, not universal culinary truth. Each recipe must be tested twice locally, measured after offering and adjusted from actual yield and leftovers. |

## 9.4 Quantity-change rules

• The Wednesday headcount produces a planning tier; the Thursday message locks it.

• A family does not independently increase the tier because “more is always better.”

• Known late guests may be absorbed by the 10% buffer; larger changes require Operations approval.

• Leftover percentage is recorded by preparation, not guessed.

• After two cycles, recipe yields are revised based on evidence.

# 10. Ingredient standard

## 10.1 Prohibited or excluded ingredients

• Meat, fish and eggs.

• Onion, garlic, shallots, scallions, chives and leeks, including powders and extracts.

• Alcohol, cooking wine and alcohol-containing flavouring unless specifically resolved by worship authority.

• Gelatin or other animal-derived additives; animal-rennet cheese.

• Packaged sauce, stock, paste or spice mix with unknown or noncompliant ingredients.

• Unpasteurised dairy, raw sprouts, low-acid home-canned food and other avoidable high-risk ingredients.

• Peanuts, tree nuts and sesame in the default pilot menus. This reduces exposure but does not create an allergen-free environment.

• Hing/asafoetida in the default pilot recipes unless the product and wheat status are verified and the recipe specifically authorises it.

## 10.2 Packaged-product verification

The cook reads the full ingredient and “contains” statements every time. Manufacturers can change formulations.

**☐ **Spice blends and masalas.

**☐ **Vegetable stock, sauces and pastes.

**☐ **Frozen vegetables with seasoning.

**☐ **Yogurt, paneer, cheese and commercial dairy products.

**☐ **Asafoetida compounds, which commonly use wheat flour.

**☐ **Bread, crackers, desserts and festival products.

**☐ **Oils shared with allergen-containing products.

|  | **Label honesty  **Do not call a dish “gluten-free,” “nut-free” or “allergen-free” unless both ingredients and cross-contact controls support that statement. “Prepared without X as an ingredient” is a narrower and more honest claim. |
| --- | --- |

## 10.3 Default pilot ingredient posture

| **Category** | **Default** |
| --- | --- |
| Cooking oil | Canola or sunflower oil; single-ingredient, clearly labelled |
| Spice level | Mild; measured from recipe card |
| Commercial masala | Not used in pilot recipes |
| Nuts / sesame | Not used in standard communal recipes |
| Dairy | Used only when declared, pasteurised and temperature-controlled |
| Fresh garnish | Washed, controlled and added before offering; no bare-hand contact |
| Salt | Measured; iodised or approved standard |
| Water | Potable water from approved source |

# 11. Allergy and dietary-management system

Figure 3. Severe allergy management requires an explicit decision, not an informal promise.

## 11.1 Required family information

• Participant and parent/guardian names.

• Allergen or intolerance and severity.

• Known symptoms and cross-contact sensitivity.

• Whether food from shared home kitchens is acceptable.

• Emergency medication, location and current action plan.

• Emergency contacts and date reviewed.

• Other dietary restrictions that affect the shared menu.

## 11.2 Severe-allergy operating rule

For a potentially life-threatening allergy, leadership and the family must choose one documented approach:

| **Option** | **Use when** | **Control** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A — Verified communal control | The group can actually control the ingredient and cross-contact risk | Written controls; weekly verification; medication and plan immediately accessible. |
| B — Sealed personal meal | Multiple home kitchens make cross-contact control unreliable | Participant’s family provides a clearly labelled, separate meal; no shared utensils. |
| C — No communal meal | Neither A nor B can be made safe | Participant may attend the program but does not consume communal food until resolved. |

## 11.3 Major allergen awareness

U.S. federal labelling recognises nine major food allergens: milk, egg, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans and sesame. KUTUMBA’s vegetarian standard excludes several by doctrine, but milk, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soy and sesame still require attention.

## 11.4 Privacy

Medical and allergy data is restricted to authorised leaders and relevant food-service adults. It is not stored in Git, displayed in a public roster or discussed as casual group information. Weekly notices state operational controls without unnecessary diagnosis details.

# 12. Food-handler health and hygiene

## 12.1 Illness exclusion

|  | **48-hour rule  **A person with vomiting or diarrhoea does not prepare, transport, offer, serve or clean food, and remains excluded for at least 48 hours after symptoms stop. The family reports the issue without embarrassment and the backup system activates. |
| --- | --- |

## 12.2 Handwashing and ready-to-eat food

• Wash hands with soap and running water before food work, after restroom use, after handling waste, after touching face or phone and whenever contamination may have occurred.

• Gloves are task-specific and changed when contaminated; they do not replace handwashing.

• Use clean utensils, deli tissue or gloves to avoid bare-hand contact with ready-to-eat food.

• Cover cuts with a waterproof dressing and a glove where appropriate.

• No cooking while simultaneously supervising small children or pets in the food-preparation area.

• Phones remain outside the active preparation zone or are handled only after handwashing.

## 12.3 Home-kitchen preparation declaration

Each Primary Cook confirms on Friday that the cook was free of exclusion symptoms, the kitchen and utensils were clean, pets were kept out of active preparation, ingredients matched the approved recipe and no one tasted the bhoga.

# 13. Weekly communication and escalation

Figure 4. The weekly control cycle resolves uncertainty before Friday.

## 13.1 Sunday roster reminder

• Date and active-week number.

• All station assignments and named leads.

• Menu code and portion tier.

• Known allergy-control notice.

• Arrival times and equipment owner.

• Primary and escalation contacts.

## 13.2 Tuesday confirmation

|  | **Deadline  **Every station confirms by Tuesday at 8:00 p.m. At 8:15 p.m. one direct reminder is sent. At 9:00 p.m. no response is treated as unavailable. By 9:30 p.m. the Operations Lead activates the replacement plan. |
| --- | --- |

The Primary Cook confirms menu, ingredient compliance, illness status, transport capacity and expected arrival. The Backup confirms emergency-menu readiness, warmers and thermometer availability. Other stations confirm attendance and role readiness.

## 13.3 Wednesday and Thursday locks

| **Control point** | **Deadline** | **Output** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Headcount and allergy update | Wednesday 6:00 p.m. | Adult/teen/child/guest count; changed dietary or allergy information. |
| Quantity and equipment lock | Thursday 8:00 p.m. | Tier A/B/C; approved substitutions; equipment assignment; final arrival plan. |
| No-surprise rule | After Thursday lock | No new dish, major substitution or unverified packaged product without approval. |

## 13.4 Emergency escalation ladder

| **When primary cancels** | **Response** |
| --- | --- |
| Before Wednesday evening | Backup attempts scheduled menu; other station reassigned. |
| Thursday or Friday before 2:00 p.m. | Use E1 or E2; cancel optional item. |
| After 2:00 p.m. | Reduced emergency meal, fruit/light service, or no meal. Notify families to eat beforehand if needed. |
| Food arrives unsafe or noncompliant | Isolate affected item; use emergency food or serve fewer items; no public shaming. |
| Venue/power/water problem | Apply approved continuity plan or cancel food service/program. Do not improvise around a basic control. |

|  | **Restaurant rescue is not automatic  **Outside food may contain onion, garlic, allergens or unclear ingredients and may not fit the approved offering process. Use only a pre-verified supplier or a separately approved emergency protocol. |

# 15. Transport, temperature and equipment control

Figure 6. KUTUMBA uses 140°F/40°F internal targets, with 135°F/41°F as the FDA/PA model-code holding limits.

## 15.1 Temperature standards

| **Control** | **KUTUMBA target** | **Action boundary** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hot holding | 140°F or above | 135–139°F: correct and recheck; below 135°F: determine time and reheat/discard. |
| Cold holding | 40°F or below | 41°F is the model-code ceiling; above 41°F: determine time and correct/discard. |
| Reheating cooked/cooled TCS food for hot holding | 165°F for 15 seconds, reached rapidly | Use stovetop, oven or microwave; a warmer is not a reheater. |
| Room-temperature exposure | Use a strict two-hour cumulative operating limit | One hour where ambient temperature exceeds 90°F. |
| Cooling retained leftovers | Shallow containers; refrigerate promptly | Do not place a deep hot pot in a crowded refrigerator and assume it cools safely. |

## 15.2 Four temperature checks

• Departure from the cook’s home.

• Arrival at the venue.

• Mid-hold during the program.

• Immediately before serving.

The probe is cleaned and sanitised between foods. The reading is taken from the thermal centre and, for large containers, more than one location.

## 15.3 Corrective decision tree

| **Observation** | **Action** |
| --- | --- |
| Hot food ≥140°F | Accept and hold. Recheck on schedule. |
| Hot food 135–139°F | Accept only as corrective buffer; raise holding performance and recheck within 15 minutes. |
| Hot food <135°F, known to be out of control less than 2 hours | Rapidly reheat to 165°F for 15 seconds using proper cooking equipment; then hold ≥140°F. |
| Hot food <135°F, time unknown or ≥2 hours under KUTUMBA rule | Do not serve; discard respectfully. |
| Cold food ≤40°F | Accept and hold. |
| Cold food 41°F, brief verified exposure | Correct immediately and recheck. |
| Cold food >41°F, time unknown or ≥2 hours under KUTUMBA rule | Do not serve; discard. |
| Thermometer absent or unreliable | Use a verified backup. If temperature cannot be verified, the item does not proceed. |

## 15.4 Transport requirements

• Preheat insulated hot carriers and pre-chill cold carriers where appropriate.

• Keep containers closed, upright, food-grade and secured against tipping.

• Separate food from chemicals, shoes, pets and unrelated cargo.

• Do not rely on a car cabin temperature to hold food safely.

• Use direct transport; avoid errands between cooking and the venue.

• Log departure time and temperature on every TCS container group.

## 15.5 Warming and electrical safety

• Verify each warmer can maintain the required temperature with actual food; appliance names are not evidence.

• Place warmers on heat-resistant mats in an adult-only zone.

• Follow manufacturer instructions and venue/fire requirements.

• No daisy-chained power strips, overloaded circuits, cords under rugs or cords across child walkways.

• Use cable covers or secured routing approved by the venue.

• Open-flame chafers are not part of the pilot unless explicitly permitted and controlled.

• One adult is assigned to equipment checks; children and teens do not troubleshoot electrical equipment.

## 16.2 Handoff sequence

• Primary Cook transfers containers directly to Operations and Bhoga Support.

• Container count, menu code, ingredient declaration and no-tasting status are confirmed.

• Arrival temperatures are measured and recorded.

• Approved offering portions and worship-only utensils are prepared.

• Only the locally authorised person performs the offering according to the approved SOP.

• Worship Lead confirms completion and the green label is applied.

• Food moves immediately to controlled holding.

• No person samples or serves food before the green status is confirmed.

This manual does not independently prescribe mantras, altar placement, offering duration, Deity-specific procedure, whether a complete batch or representative plate is offered, or who is qualified to perform the offering. # 17. Serving, hospitality and child seating

## 17.1 Serving order

After the approved prasāda prayer: participants with documented dietary arrangements; young children with parents; other children; adults; then servers after the first pass. Moderate first portions protect fairness and reduce waste.

## 17.2 Serving controls

• One clean, dedicated utensil per preparation.

• Used personal utensils never enter common food containers.

• Children do not self-serve from hot vessels.

• Ingredient/allergen cards remain visible at the line.

• Water is available before service begins.

• Seconds begin only after everyone has a first opportunity.

• Ready-to-eat garnish and fruit are handled with utensils or clean gloves.

• No comments on another participant’s portion, weight, diet or appetite.

## 17.3 Hospitality standard

• Welcome guests without interrogating spiritual background or food choices.

• Explain prasāda etiquette briefly and naturally.

• Do not pressure anyone to eat a preparation that conflicts with their health or documented plan.

• Do not turn late arrival into a public correction.

• Treat servers and cleaners as participants, not invisible labour.

• Close the formal evening on time; hospitality does not require uncontrolled lingering.

## 17.4 Child seating

| **Group** | **Pilot standard** |
| --- | --- |
| Under six | Seated with parent or designated caregiver; away from warmers, cords and serving traffic. |
| Age 6–12 | May sit together only with two adults visibly nearby and parents in the room; adults serve hot food. |
| Teens | May distribute plates, reset chairs and count dry inventory; do not own temperature, hot vessels, allergy response or younger-child safety. |
| Choking controls | Parents manage age-appropriate food; do not serve whole grapes, hard candy or similar avoidable hazards to young children. |

• Assign adults to dishes, tables/chairs, waste/floors and asset reset.

• Primary Cook family is exempt from leading cleanup.

## 18.3 Leftover hierarchy

| **Priority** | **Action** |
| --- | --- |

| 2 | Package safe remaining prasāda promptly in clean, shallow, labelled containers. |
| 3 | Distribute only when time/temperature history is known and safe. |
| 4 | Refrigerate promptly where the venue permits and the recipient can maintain control. |
| 5 | Discard food when time, temperature, contamination or allergen status is uncertain. |

## 18.4 Take-home label

Every retained container states preparation, date/time packed, known allergens, “keep refrigerated,” and “reheat to 165°F.” Routine carry-forward to the next KUTUMBA Friday is prohibited.

# 23. Incident response and continuity

## 23.1 Food-safety incident

• Stop service of affected food.

• Isolate and label it “HOLD — DO NOT SERVE.”

• Record time, temperature, ingredients, persons and actions.

• Preserve packaging/labels where relevant.

• Use emergency meal or reduce service.

• Seek medical or regulatory assistance when indicated.

• Review before the next active week without public blame.

## 23.2 Allergy incident

• Follow the participant’s current emergency action plan.

• Use emergency services when indicated; do not delay for group discussion.

• Notify parent/guardian.

• Preserve ingredient and cross-contact evidence.

• Document privately.

• Suspend the relevant recipe/control until reviewed.

## 23.3 Suspected foodborne illness after the program

• Collect facts without diagnosing: people affected, symptoms, timing and foods consumed.

• Tell affected families to seek appropriate medical advice.

• Preserve recipe, labels, temperature logs and leftovers only when safe to do so.

• Notify venue/regulator according to applicable requirements.

• Pause implicated recipes or processes.

• Do not use group chat for speculation or blame.

## 23.4 Continuity priorities

| --- | --- |
| 1 | Human safety and medical response |
| 2 | Worship integrity and honest food status |
| 3 | Clear family communication |
| 4 | A reduced but orderly program |
| 5 | Meal completeness and convenience |

**2. **Wash under potable running water and dry with clean single-use material.

**3. **Do not cut fruit unless cold holding and clean ready-to-eat handling are controlled.

**4. **Avoid choking hazards for young children; parents portion as needed.

## Holding and transport

Whole intact fruit follows product-specific storage. Cut fruit is TCS in many cases and must be held ≤40°F.

## Research interpretation rules

• The FDA Food Code is a model code; the applicable Pennsylvania/local authority and venue determine enforceable requirements.

• KUTUMBA uses stricter 140°F/40°F internal targets for operational clarity while recognising 135°F/41°F model-code holding limits.

• The strict two-hour cumulative exposure rule is a conservative program control; local law may permit other documented time-as-control procedures that require formal setup and marking.

• Allergen avoidance in a recipe does not prove absence of cross-contact in a private home kitchen.

• ISKCON sources justify the devotional boundary; exact procedures are not copied into this operations manual and must be locally approved.
