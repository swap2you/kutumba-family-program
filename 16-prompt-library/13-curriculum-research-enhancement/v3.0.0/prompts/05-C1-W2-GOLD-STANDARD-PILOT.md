# Prompt 05 — C1-W2 Gold-Standard Pilot: “I Am Not This Body”

## Objective

Transform C1-W2 into the reference implementation for all remaining modules.

Do not batch the other weeks until this pilot passes Prompt 06.

## Controlling learning boundary

C1-W2 teaches the distinction between the changing body and the continuing conscious self.

It does **not** attempt to exhaust:

- all qualities of the soul;
- full transmigration mechanics;
- subtle-body cosmology;
- karma;
- Paramātmā;
- acintya-bhedābheda;
- liberation;
- rasa.

Those topics belong to later modules. Cross-link them without swallowing them.

## Initial primary source matrix

At minimum research and verify:

- Bhagavad-gītā 2.13 — continuity through bodily stages;
- Bhagavad-gītā 2.16 — changing and enduring realities;
- Bhagavad-gītā 2.20 — the soul does not undergo bodily transformations;
- Bhagavad-gītā 2.22 — garment analogy;
- Bhagavad-gītā 13.1–3 — field and knower, using the correct edition numbering;
- Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 10.84.13 — error of bodily self-identification;
- Caitanya-caritāmṛta Madhya 20.108–109 — constitutional identity and eternal service;
- directly relevant verified Śrīla Prabhupāda lectures and conversations.

Use additional references only when they add a distinct teaching function.

## Required research questions

1. What exactly changes?
2. What remains continuous?
3. What evidence or reasoning is used in Śrīla Prabhupāda’s teaching?
4. What does “I am not this body” **not** mean?
5. How does this teaching affect:
   - body image;
   - aging;
   - disability;
   - illness;
   - race and ethnicity;
   - gendered safety;
   - profession and status;
   - animal compassion;
   - care for trees and other living beings;
   - medical care;
   - grief;
   - spiritual bypassing?
6. What belongs in C1-W3 instead?
7. What can a four-year-old understand?
8. What can a fourteen-year-old critically examine?
9. What should a parent practice this week?
10. Which analogies are scriptural, which are Śrīla Prabhupāda’s, and which are KUTUMBA-created?

## Required analogies

Develop and source-tag at least four, such as:

- childhood–youth–old age continuity;
- changing garments;
- driver and vehicle, with limitations clearly stated;
- observer and observed;
- house and resident;
- device and user, marked as a modern pedagogical analogy.

Every analogy must include:

- teaching value;
- limitation;
- age suitability;
- source status;
- risk of misuse;
- practical family example.

## Required contemporary case studies

Create anonymized cases, not gossip:

- social-media appearance pressure;
- a parent fearing aging;
- a child mocking disability;
- a devotee neglecting sleep or medicine under “not the body” language;
- professional-status collapse;
- prejudice based on body;
- cruelty toward animals or plants;
- a bereaved family hearing the teaching insensitively.

Each case must include:

- presenting situation;
- mistaken conclusion;
- source-grounded correction;
- compassionate response;
- facilitator caution;
- Lāla–Lālī adaptation;
- Kiśora–Kiśorī adaptation.

## Lāla–Lālī, ages 4–8

Create:

- one 8–10 minute story;
- body-change photo or drawing sequence;
- costume/name-card activity;
- “same person” thread craft;
- body-care sorting activity;
- one simple Sanskrit phrase;
- one recall line;
- one movement break;
- one take-home activity;
- core version for ages 4–6;
- extension for ages 7–8.

Do not teach frightening death imagery.

## Kiśora–Kiśorī, ages 9–14

Create:

- text observation;
- social-media identity case;
- respectful debate;
- body/self comparison diagram;
- one short reflective writing exercise;
- disability and dignity application;
- service-based identity statement;
- core version for ages 9–11;
- extension for ages 12–14.

Do not force personal disclosure.

- home application;
- misconception check;
- closing saṅkalpa.
